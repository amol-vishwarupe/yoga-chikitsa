Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana — the Extended Big Toe Pose — is a standing balance in which the practitioner stands on one leg, extends the other leg forward (or to the side), and grasps the big toe of the extended foot. It simultaneously develops single-leg balance, hamstring and hip flexibility, and the focused concentration that sustained standing balance demands. It appears in both the Ashtanga Primary Series and as a standalone standing balance in all yoga traditions.

What is Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana?

Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana — pronounced oo-TEE-tah HAHS-tah pah-dahn-goosh-TAHS-ana — translates as Extended Hand to Big Toe Pose (Utthita = extended, Hasta = hand, Pada = foot, Angustha = big toe, Asana = posture). The pose is performed on both sides: the standing leg bears the body’s weight while the opposite leg is held at hip height or above, with the hand gripping the extended foot’s big toe in a Padangustha grip.

The pose encompasses two primary expressions — the forward extension and the lateral extension — each challenging balance and flexibility differently. Like Virabhadrasana III and Vrksasana, Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana develops the single-leg stability and focused gaze (drishti) that define advanced standing balance practice. At Habuild, both extensions are taught progressively — beginning with the forward extension before introducing the lateral variation.

Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana Benefits

Physical Benefits

Builds Single-Leg Balance and Proprioception
Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana places the complete demand of balance on one leg — developing the vestibular, proprioceptive, and neuromuscular coordination of the standing leg’s hip, knee, and ankle stabilisers. This single-leg balance development directly supports

yoga for flexibility and general movement quality in daily activity.
Stretches the Hamstrings and Hip Flexors

The extended leg requires both hamstring length (for the forward extension) and hip abductor flexibility (for the lateral extension). Progressive Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana practice provides meaningful hamstring and hip opening alongside the balance training — complementing seated forward fold practices like
Paschimottanasana with the added balance challenge.

Strengthens the Standing Leg and Core
The standing leg’s glute medius, quadriceps, and ankle stabilisers work continuously throughout the pose — the sustained single-leg load progressively building the lower limb strength and ankle stability that injury prevention and daily function require.

Improves Hip Abductor Strength in the Lateral Variation
The lateral extension of Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana — leg held to the side at hip height — specifically activates the standing leg’s hip abductors and the extended leg’s hip abductors simultaneously. This bilateral hip abductor work is a meaningful complement to

Trikonasana for comprehensive lateral hip strength and stability.

Mental and Emotional Benefits

Develops Drishti and Single-Pointed Concentration
The sustained balance demand of Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana requires unwavering drishti (fixed gaze) — any distraction of the gaze immediately disrupts the balance. This pose is among yoga’s most direct practices for developing the single-pointed concentration that both meditation and high-performance daily focus require.

Builds Equanimity Under Physical Demand
Holding a challenging balance while maintaining even breathing and composed expression develops the quality of equanimity — the calm, non-reactive presence — that distinguishes advanced yoga practice from mere physical exercise.

How to Do Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana — Step-by-Step

Key Principles

The standing leg is active throughout — never lock the knee but engage the quadriceps and keep the hip over the ankle. The gaze (drishti) is fixed at a single unmoving point — changing the gaze topples the balance. The spine stays long — do not round the back to reach the foot. If the hamstrings do not allow the full grip, use a strap around the arch of the extended foot.

Step 1: Establish Tadasana
Stand in Tadasana — feet together, weight even, spine tall, gaze steady at a fixed point at eye level.

Step 2: Lift the Right Knee
Inhale and lift the right knee to hip height — thigh parallel to the floor. Hold the right knee with the right hand momentarily. Establish balance on the left leg.

Step 3: Grip the Big Toe and Extend Forward
Loop the right index and middle fingers around the right big toe (Padangustha grip). Exhale and extend the right leg forward — straightening the knee as much as hamstring flexibility allows. Left hand on the left hip. Hold for 5 breaths.

Step 4: Open the Leg to the Right (Lateral Variation)
Inhale. On an exhale, open the right leg to the right — extending it to the side at hip height. Turn the gaze to the left. Left arm may extend to the left for counterbalance. Hold for 5 breaths.

Step 5: Return to Centre and Release
Inhale and bring the right leg back to centre (forward extension). Exhale and release the grip, lower the right knee, and return to Tadasana. Repeat on the left side.

Breathing in Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana

Each transition is initiated on an inhale (creating length and space) and settled on an exhale (grounding the balance). During the held positions, breathe slowly and evenly — the quality of breath directly reflects and influences the quality of the balance. Laboured breathing indicates too much muscular tension; a steady, quiet breath reflects genuine postural composure.

Preparatory Poses

Supta Padangusthasana — supine big toe pose builds hamstring length before the standing version Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana combines single-leg balance, hamstring flexibility, hip opening, and drishti concentration in one demanding but accessible standing pose. Its strap variation makes it immediately available; its full expression develops over weeks of consistent practice. Frequently Asked Questions

What is Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana?

Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana (Extended Big Toe Pose) is a standing balance in which one leg is held extended at hip height with the hand gripping the big toe. It develops single-leg balance, hamstring and hip flexibility, and single-pointed concentration through the drishti practice it demands.

What are the benefits of Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana?

Benefits include single-leg balance and proprioceptive development, hamstring and hip flexor lengthening, standing leg and core strengthening, hip abductor strengthening in the lateral variation, and the focused drishti concentration that sustained standing balance requires. These benefits develop progressively with consistent practice.

What are the variations of Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana?

The primary variations are: with strap (beginner — full extension maintained without complete hamstring flexibility); forward extension with toe grip (intermediate — standard entry expression); and the complete forward-then-lateral sequence (full expression — both extensions held at 5 breaths each, as in the Ashtanga Primary Series).

How do you do Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana for beginners?

Begin with a yoga strap looped around the arch of the extended foot. Stand in Tadasana, lift the opposite knee, loop the strap, and extend the leg forward — keeping the spine long and the gaze fixed. Hold for 5 breaths. Practise the forward extension only initially before introducing the lateral variation.

Is Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana the same as Extended Big Toe Pose?

Yes — Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana and Extended Big Toe Pose (or Extended Hand to Big Toe Pose) are the same posture — Sanskrit and English names for the identical standing balance. The shorter term ‘hasta padangusthasana’ also refers to the same pose and its variations.
