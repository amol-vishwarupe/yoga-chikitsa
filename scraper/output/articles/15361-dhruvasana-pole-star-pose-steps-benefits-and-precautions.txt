What is Dhruvasana?

Dhruvasana — pronounced DHRU-vah-sah-nah — derives from the Sanskrit dhruva (fixed, immovable, the Pole Star or North Star) and asana (pose). The English name is Pole Star Pose — named after the North Star (Polaris), the celestial body that remains fixed while all other stars appear to rotate around it. This cosmic symbolism is the essence of Dhruvasana: the practitioner stands steady and immovable on one leg, like the Pole Star, while the world around them is in motion.

Dhruvasana — the Pole Star Pose — is a single-leg balance and meditation pose named after Dhruva, the child who performed such intense tapas (disciplined practice) that he was granted the position of the immovable Pole Star in Hindu cosmology. The pose embodies the quality of Dhruva’s practice: unwavering, centred and unshakeable. This complete guide covers dhruvasana benefits, the technique that builds this quality of stability, and the full progression from beginner to classical expression.

Dhruvasana is a standing one-legged balance pose in which the practitioner balances on one foot while the other leg is raised (in various traditions, to the thigh in a Vrikshasana-like position, or extended forward or backward). In some Hatha yoga traditions, Dhruvasana is identical to or closely related to Vrikshasana (Tree Pose), while in others it refers specifically to a standing balance with the raised leg extended horizontally or forward. The common element across all Dhruvasana interpretations is the quality of Dhruva — the fixed, unmoving steadiness of the Pole Star.

The traditional story of Dhruva is one of yoga’s most inspiring — a young boy who, rejected and humiliated, performed intense tapas (austerities) standing on one leg for thousands of years, ultimately achieving the highest state of consciousness and being placed in the sky as the unmoving Pole Star. Dhruvasana honours this story: each standing balance practice is an invitation to access the Dhruva quality within — the unwavering stillness that exists beneath all external turbulence.

How to Do Dhruvasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

The three foundations of Dhruvasana: (1) the standing foot — press all four corners actively, spread the toes, activate the arch, (2) the drishti (gaze) — a fixed, unmoving focal point at eye level is essential for balance, and (3) the breath — slow and steady, never held. The quality of the standing foot and the steadiness of the gaze determine the stability of the balance more than the position of the raised leg.

Step 1: Establish Samasthiti (Equal Standing)

Begin in Samasthiti (Equal Standing Pose) — feet together, weight evenly distributed, spine upright, gaze forward. Take 5 breaths here to establish the centred, grounded quality of the Pole Star from which the one-legged balance will arise. Feel the steadiness of two-foot standing as the reference point for the single-leg challenge.

Step 2: Choose the Drishti — Fixed Gaze Point

Before lifting any foot, choose a fixed, unmoving focal point at eye level — a point on the wall, a spot on the floor slightly ahead. This drishti (gaze) is the anchor of the balance. The gaze must remain fixed and unmoving throughout the entire standing balance. This step alone improves balance by 30-40% compared to unfocused or shifting gaze.

Step 3: Shift Weight Onto the Standing Foot

Slowly shift the entire body weight onto the right foot — pressing all four corners of the right foot firmly into the floor. Feel the ankle stabilisers engaging as the full weight comes onto one foot. The standing foot is active and engaged — not passive. Take 2-3 breaths here with full weight on one foot before lifting the other.

Step 4: Raise the Opposite Leg

On an exhalation, raise the left foot — either placing it against the right inner thigh (Vrikshasana-style) or extending it forward or to the side depending on the tradition followed. The standing leg remains straight (not locked), the hip of the raised leg remains level, and the spine remains upright. Hands may be at the heart in Anjali Mudra or raised overhead.

Step 5: Full Dhruvasana — Pole Star Balance

In the full Dhruvasana, the standing leg is stable and active, the raised leg is in its chosen position, the spine is elongated and upright, the gaze is fixed on the unmoving drishti and the breath is slow and steady. Hold for 5-10 slow breaths — embodying the Dhruva quality of unwavering steadiness. The wobbling and micro-corrections are not failure; they are the practice.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Dhruvasana

On an exhalation, lower the raised foot slowly and with control back to Samasthiti. Take 3 centring breaths in Samasthiti before performing the identical balance on the opposite side. Equal time on both sides is essential — the asymmetry of one-sided practice creates the imbalances that Dhruvasana is designed to reveal and correct.

Breathing in Dhruvasana

Slow, continuous nasal breathing throughout Dhruvasana. The breath is the steadiness regulator — when the breath becomes hurried or held, the balance is being maintained by tension rather than by genuine stability. Slow the breath down to slow the postural sway. The quality of the breath is the most reliable indicator of the quality of the balance.

Preparatory Poses Before Dhruvasana

Samasthiti (Equal Standing) — the foundational standing awareness from which all standing balance develops.

Single-leg standing with wall support — develops the one-leg stability before the free-standing challenge.

Warrior III (Virabhadrasana III) — develops the hip extension balance that some Dhruvasana variations require.

Vrikshasana (Tree Pose) — the most similar standing balance that shares the upright-body Dhruvasana quality.

Variations of Dhruvasana

Variation 1: Dhruvasana with Wall Support (Beginner)

Performing the one-leg balance with one hand lightly touching a wall — reduces the balance challenge while developing the standing leg strength and hip stability. Difficulty: Beginner

Variation 2: Dhruvasana with Raised Foot at Thigh (Classic)

The Vrikshasana-style Dhruvasana with the raised foot pressed against the inner thigh — the most stable and most commonly practised one-legged balance that embodies the Pole Star steadiness. Difficulty: All levels

Variation 3: Dhruvasana with Extended Leg (Advanced)

The raised leg extended forward horizontally or held by the hand in a standing leg extension — significantly increases the balance challenge and 
hip flexor
 demand compared to the thigh-pressed foot variation. Difficulty: Intermediate-Advanced

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Dhruvasana

Mistake 1: Unfixed or Wandering Gaze
A wandering drishti is the single most common cause of balance loss in Dhruvasana. The gaze must be genuinely fixed and unmoving — as immovable as the Pole Star itself. Choose the drishti before lifting the foot and commit to it completely throughout the hold.

Mistake 2: Hip of Raised Leg Hiking Upward
Allowing the hip of the raised leg side to hike upward eliminates the gluteus medius activation of the standing leg and creates spinal lateral flexion. Keep both hips level throughout the balance.

Mistake 3: Locking the Standing Knee
Hyperextending the standing knee eliminates the proprioceptive feedback from the knee joint that contributes to balance. Maintain a micro-bend in the standing knee — engaged but not locked.

Mistake 4: Holding the Breath
Breath holding during the balance creates the tension that destabilises rather than stabilises the pose. Slow, continuous breathing is the marker and the means of genuine Dhruvasana balance quality.
