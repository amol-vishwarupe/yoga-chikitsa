What is Sarvangasana?

Sarvangasana (Sanskrit: सर्वांगासन) is pronounced 
sar-VAHN-gah-sah-nah
. The name comes from three Sanskrit roots: 
sarva
 (all), 
anga
 (limb or body part), and 
asana
 (pose) — literally meaning “all-limbs pose” or the pose that benefits every part of the body. In English, it is most commonly called the Shoulder Stand.

In the pose, the practitioner lies on their back and lifts the entire lower body — legs, hips, and torso — vertically upward, supporting the weight on the shoulders and upper arms while the chin presses into the chest. The result is a full-body inversion where blood flow, breath, and internal organ activity are all reoriented. Visually, the body forms a straight, vertical line from shoulder to heel.

Within the broader yoga system, Sarvangasana holds a revered place. Classical Hatha yoga texts refer to it as the “queen of asanas” — complementing Sirsasana (the “king”). It sits comfortably in intermediate sequences but is adaptable enough for guided beginners. Its influence extends across multiple body systems simultaneously, making it uniquely suited to those working on healthy weight gain and hormonal balance.

How to Do Sarvangasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Before you begin, keep these alignment principles in mind throughout the pose: weight must rest on the shoulders and upper arms — never the neck. The chin should naturally tuck toward the chest, not be forced. Keep the legs actively engaged, not passive. Use a folded blanket under the shoulders if needed to protect the cervical spine.

Step 1: Starting Position

Lie flat on your back on a yoga mat. Extend both legs together and rest your arms alongside your body with palms facing down. Take 3–5 slow, deep breaths here to settle the body and mind. Feel the length of your spine against the floor and engage a light abdominal draw.

Step 2: Lifting the Legs

On an exhale, press your palms firmly into the floor and engage your core to lift both legs to a 90-degree angle. Keep the legs straight and together. If this is challenging, bend the knees slightly as you lift, then straighten once the legs are up. You should feel engagement through the abdominals and hip flexors.

Step 3: Rolling the Hips Up

Inhale, then on the next exhale, use your core and the press of your palms to roll your hips off the floor, continuing to swing the legs up and over toward vertical. Your lower back will lift away from the mat. This is the transition moment — move slowly and with control. Avoid using momentum or a jerking motion.

Step 4: Supporting the Back

As your hips rise and your weight shifts to the shoulders, bend your elbows and bring your hands to your lower or mid-back for support. Fingers point upward toward the hips. The elbows should be no wider than shoulder-width — actively draw them in. Feel your shoulder blades pressing together and down into the mat to create a stable base.

Step 5: Final Position and Hold

Walk your hands up the back toward the shoulder blades and extend both legs vertically toward the ceiling. The body should form one straight line from the tops of the shoulders to the tips of the toes. Chin tucks naturally toward the sternum. Gaze at your feet or the ceiling. Hold for 30 seconds initially, building gradually toward 3–5 minutes with practice. Breathe slowly and evenly throughout.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Sarvangasana

To exit, bend both knees slightly and begin to lower them toward the floor, using your core to control the descent. Bring your hands back to the floor alongside your hips and slowly roll the spine down vertebra by vertebra. Lower the legs last. Rest in Shavasana (Corpse Pose) for at least 30 seconds before sitting up. Never come out of the pose abruptly.

Breathing in Sarvangasana

Breathe in through the nose and out through the nose throughout the pose. In the inverted position, the abdominal breath is naturally restricted — allow the breath to move into the chest instead. Keep each inhale and exhale slow and steady. Avoid holding the breath. If you notice the breath becoming strained, come out of the pose and rest — it means the hold is too long for your current capacity.

Preparatory Poses Before Sarvangasana

These warm-up poses prepare the relevant muscle groups and help you enter Sarvangasana safely and with better alignment.

Setu Bandhasana (Bridge Pose)
 — opens the chest, activates the upper back, and warms up the neck and shoulder region that bears weight in Sarvangasana.

Viparita Karani (Legs-Up-The-Wall Pose)
 — a gentle inversion that acclimatises the nervous system to inverted blood flow before a full shoulder stand.

Halasana (Plough Pose)
 — often practised as a transition into Sarvangasana; it lengthens the hamstrings and the entire posterior chain.

Supta Padangusthasana (Reclining Hand-to-Toe Pose)
 — stretches the hamstrings thoroughly, making it easier to keep the legs straight once inverted.

Variations of Sarvangasana

Variation 1: Ardha Sarvangasana (Half Shoulder Stand)

Difficulty: Beginner

In this variation, the body does not reach full vertical — instead, the hips are lifted and the back is supported by the hands, but the legs angle at roughly 45–60 degrees rather than straight up. This significantly reduces the load on the neck and shoulders while still providing many of the circulatory and digestive benefits. It is the ideal starting point for beginners or anyone with neck sensitivity.

Variation 2: Eka Pada Sarvangasana (One-Legged Shoulder Stand)

Difficulty: Intermediate

From the full Sarvangasana position, one leg is slowly lowered toward the floor while the other remains vertical. This variation intensifies the core engagement and introduces an asymmetrical balance challenge. It also deepens the stretch along the inner thigh and hip flexors of the descending leg. Alternate sides evenly.

Variation 3: Niralamba Sarvangasana (Unsupported Shoulder Stand)

Difficulty: Advanced

In this demanding variation, the arms are released from the back and extended either alongside the body or raised toward the ceiling — the body balances with no hand support whatsoever. The entire weight is managed through refined core engagement and precise shoulder-blade positioning. Attempt this only after consistently holding the supported version for several minutes with stable breath.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Sarvangasana

Resting weight on the neck:
 The most common and potentially harmful error. All weight must be distributed across the shoulders and upper arms — never the cervical spine. Use a folded blanket under the shoulders to create this distance.

Elbows splaying wide:
 When the elbows drift outward, the foundation becomes unstable and the upper back collapses. Actively draw the elbows toward each other throughout the hold.

Collapsing the back instead of lifting:
 If the hands slide too low toward the waist, the back tends to curve rather than rise vertically. Walk the hands up toward the shoulder blades to maintain the lift.

Holding the breath:
 Inverted poses tempt many practitioners to unconsciously stop breathing. Keep the breath moving — steady, nasal, and slow — throughout every second of the hold.

Coming out too fast:
 Sitting straight up from an inversion rapidly shifts blood pressure and can cause dizziness. Always roll out slowly, vertebra by vertebra, and rest in Shavasana before transitioning.

Forcing the chin into the chest:
 The chin tuck in Sarvangasana should be a natural consequence of the body’s alignment, not a forced compression of the throat. If you feel strain in the throat, ease the chin back slightly.

Frequently Asked Questions About Shoulder Stand Yoga

What is Shoulder Stand yoga?

Shoulder Stand, or Sarvangasana, is a classical full-body inversion where the entire lower body is raised vertically and the weight rests on the shoulders and upper arms. It is often called the “queen of asanas” in Hatha yoga because of its wide-ranging effects on the thyroid, circulation, digestion, and nervous system.

Is Shoulder Stand yoga good for beginners?

Yes, with modifications. Beginners should start with Ardha Sarvangasana (the half version), use a folded blanket under the shoulders to protect the neck, and practise under live guidance until the alignment is stable. Rushing into the full pose without instruction is the most common cause of neck strain for new practitioners.

What is the difference between Shoulder Stand yoga and Hatha yoga?

Hatha yoga is a broad system of physical yoga practice that encompasses hundreds of asanas, pranayama techniques, and cleansing practices. Sarvangasana (Shoulder Stand) is a single asana within that system. Most Hatha yoga sequences include Sarvangasana as a key component of a balanced session.

Can Shoulder Stand yoga help with weight gain?

Sarvangasana can support healthy weight gain by stimulating the thyroid gland, improving digestive efficiency and nutrient absorption, building lean muscular tone, and reducing cortisol levels that can otherwise suppress appetite and anabolic processes. It is not a stand-alone solution, but it is a meaningful and targeted part of a yoga practice designed with weight gain in mind.

How many calories does Shoulder Stand yoga burn?

Sarvangasana is a low-intensity hold, so caloric burn is modest — typically 3–6 calories per minute during the hold itself. Its value in a weight gain context lies not in calorie expenditure but in the hormonal, digestive, and muscular benefits it provides over consistent, regular practice.

How often should I practise Shoulder Stand yoga?

For weight gain support and general benefit, practising Sarvangasana 4–6 times per week as part of a broader yoga sequence is a reasonable target. Start with shorter holds of 30–60 seconds and gradually build toward 3–5 minutes over several weeks. Stretchy leggings or fitted shorts and a snug top work well. Practise barefoot on a non-slip yoga mat.
