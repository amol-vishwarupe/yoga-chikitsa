What is Raja Kapotasana?

Raja Kapotasana derives from Sanskrit raja (king or royal), kapota (pigeon or dove) and asana (pose). The English name is King Pigeon Pose — the royal full expression of the Pigeon family. The “king” designation indicates this is the most complete, most demanding expression of the kapota family — with the back leg bent, foot held overhead and torso in a deep backbend reaching toward the raised foot.

Raja Kapotasana — the King Pigeon Pose — is yoga’s most comprehensive anterior chain opening pose: a full expression of the Pigeon family that combines the deep hip external rotation and psoas stretch of Eka Pada Rajakapotasana with a full spinal backbend that reaches the foot of the bent-back leg toward the head. It is one of the most coveted and most demanding poses in the Hatha yoga repertoire, representing the integration of hip flexibility, spinal extension and the courage to open completely. This guide covers the full systematic progression.

Raja Kapotasana and Eka Pada Raja Kapotasana are related but distinct poses. The eka pada raja kapotasana (one-legged version) is more commonly taught: one leg extended back, the other knee bent forward, in a hip opener without the extreme backbend. Full Raja Kapotasana adds the deep backbend — the back leg is bent and the foot drawn overhead while the torso arches dramatically backward to meet it. The word “raja” (king) designates this extreme full expression.

How to Do Raja Kapotasana (Eka Pada) — Step-by-Step

Key Principles

Begin with Eka Pada Raja Kapotasana (hip opener) before approaching full Raja Kapotasana (extreme backbend). Full Raja Kapotasana requires years of progressive backbend preparation — Ustrasana, Bhekasana and Kapotasana must be firmly established first. The instructions below cover the Eka Pada version with full Raja Kapotasana as the advanced progression.

Step 1: Begin in Low Lunge

From 
Downward Dog
, step the right foot forward between the hands. Lower into a low lunge — right knee over ankle, left knee on the floor.

Step 2: Bring Right Knee Forward — Pigeon Position

Slide the right knee toward the right wrist — the right shin moving toward horizontal across the mat. The right foot moves toward the left wrist. Allow the right hip to lower toward the floor. This is the fundamental pigeon hip opener — one of the deepest available hip external rotation stretches in yoga.

Step 3: Extend Left Leg Back — Square the Hips

Extend the left leg directly back, top of left foot pressing into the floor. Square the hips as much as possible toward the front of the mat, using a blanket under the right hip if needed. The squared hips maximise the hip opening and protect the sacroiliac joint.

Step 4: Torso Upright — Eka Pada Raja Kapotasana

Press hands into the floor and rise the torso upright — spine elongated. For the eka pada raja kapotasana, begin to arch backward from the thoracic spine, hands walking back toward the hips. This is the ekapada raja kapotasana upright expression.

Step 5: Bend Back Knee — Full Raja Kapotasana Begins

For full Raja Kapotasana, bend the left knee drawing the left foot toward the head. The thoracic arch deepens as the foot rises. Reach the arms overhead and back to hold the raised foot. The crown moves toward the foot — closing the extreme spinal extension arc. Only attempt with a fully warm spine under teacher guidance.

Step 6: Come Out and Switch Sides

Release the back foot carefully and return to the floor. Rest in the prone pigeon position for 5 breaths, then transition through Downward Dog before performing the opposite side. After full Raja Kapotasana, rest in 
Child’s Pose
 for 
3-5 minutes
 to allow spinal neutralisation.

Breathing in Raja Kapotasana

In Eka Pada Raja Kapotasana, full 
diaphragmatic
 breathing — each exhalation deepening the hip opening. In the full backbend, maintain slow shallow nasal breathing throughout the extreme extension. Never hold the breath in the full expression.

Preparatory Poses

Utthan Pristhasana (Lizard Pose) — deepens hip flexor opening prerequisite.

Bhekasana (Frog Pose) — opens quadriceps and anterior hip for the back leg bend.

Ustrasana (Camel Pose) — develops thoracic extension for the full backbend.

Eka Pada Raja Kapotasana hip opener — must be fully established before adding any backbend component.

Variations

Variation 1: Eka Pada Raja Kapotasana — Forward Fold Hip Opener (Beginner-Intermediate)

Pigeon position with torso in forward fold over the front shin — most accessible and most commonly taught version. Focuses primarily on hip external rotation without backbend. Difficulty: Beginner-Intermediate

Variation 2: Eka Pada Raja Kapotasana — Upright Torso (Intermediate)

Pigeon with upright torso and modest backbend — developing the hip-plus-backbend combination before the back knee is bent. Difficulty: Intermediate

Variation 3: Full Raja Kapotasana — Foot to Head (Expert)

Complete king pigeon with back foot drawn to crown of head in extreme spinal extension — requires years of preparatory backbend development. Difficulty: Expert

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Unsquared Hips in Pigeon Base
Allowing one hip to collapse and the other to rise creates SI joint misalignment. Use a blanket under the lower hip to maintain the squared-hip position throughout.

Mistake 2: Approaching Full Backbend Without Prerequisites
The full Raja Kapotasana backbend requires years of progressive preparation. Approaching without established Ustrasana, Bhekasana and Kapotasana creates risk of lumbar compression and cervical strain.

Mistake 3: Holding Breath During Backbend Phase
Breath holding creates tension preventing progressive deepening. Maintain slow continuous breathing throughout the full raja kapotasana backbend despite restricted respiratory depth.

Mistake 4: Insufficient Time in Hip Opener Phase
Rushing through the hip opener to reach the backbend misses the primary therapeutic value — the hip external rotation opening. Hold the hip opener for 2-5 minutes each side before adding any backbend component.
