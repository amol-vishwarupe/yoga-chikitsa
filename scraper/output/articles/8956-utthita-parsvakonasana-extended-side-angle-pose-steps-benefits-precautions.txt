Utthita Parsvakonasana — the Extended Side Angle Pose — is one of yoga’s most comprehensive standing strength poses, simultaneously building leg and core endurance, opening the lateral body from heel to fingertips, stimulating the abdominal organs, and developing the grounded power that the warrior family of poses embodies. It bridges the structural strength of Virabhadrasana II with the lateral reach of Trikonasana in a pose that demands both sustained effort and full extension simultaneously.

What is Utthita Parsvakonasana?

Utthita Parsvakonasana — pronounced oo-TEE-tah parsh-vah-cone-AHS-ana — translates as Extended (Utthita) Side (Parsva) Angle (Kona) Pose. The defining characteristic is the single unbroken diagonal line — extending from the outer heel of the back foot through the outer hip, lateral ribcage, and shoulder to the fingertips of the raised arm — creating the maximum lateral body length that the bent-knee standing architecture allows.

In the utthita parsvakonasana pose, the practitioner stands in a wide-legged stance with the front knee bent to 90 degrees and the back leg straight and strong. The front arm reaches to the floor or block beside the front foot, and the back arm extends overhead in the direction of the front foot — creating the full diagonal from heel to fingertip.

Utthita Parsvakonasana Benefits

Physical Benefits

Builds Leg, Glute, and Core Strength Through Sustained Effort
The 90-degree front knee bend sustained throughout Utthita Parsvakonasana demands intense quadriceps, glute, and lateral core engagement — building the functional lower body and core endurance that supports every other athletic and yoga movement. The sustained hold distinguishes this pose as a strength builder rather than just a stretch.

Creates Maximum Lateral Body Opening from Heel to Fingertip
The single diagonal line of Utthita Parsvakonasana opens the outer hip, lateral ribcage, intercostal spaces, and anterior shoulder simultaneously. This comprehensive lateral opening is particularly valuable for those managing yoga for posture goals and the lateral spinal imbalances that asymmetric daily habits create.

Stimulates the Digestive Organs and Abdominal Function
The lateral compression and extension of the abdomen in Utthita Parsvakonasana massages the digestive organs directly — improving abdominal circulation and relieving the bloating and digestive stagnation that sedentary habits and poor lateral mobility produce.

Develops Standing Endurance and Physical Stamina
Utthita Parsvakonasana is one of yoga’s most physically demanding standing holds — the combination of strength, extension, and breath control building the sustained physical stamina that carries into all daily and athletic movement.

Mental and Emotional Benefits

Builds Determination, Grit, and Grounded Effort
The sustained effort of holding Utthita Parsvakonasana — legs strong, lateral body open, breath steady — builds the quality of determined, grounded effort that the warrior poses embody. Regular practice cultivates the willingness to stay present with physical discomfort that extends directly into how practitioners approach daily challenge.

Improves Focus and Body Awareness
The precise alignment demands — front knee over front ankle, back leg straight, unbroken diagonal line — require sustained attentional focus that builds body awareness and present-moment concentration. Combined with dedicated yoga for concentration practice, the standing pose training significantly sharpens physical and mental attention.

How to Do Utthita Parsvakonasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

The front knee stays directly over the front ankle throughout — not collapsing inward. The back leg remains completely straight and strong. The torso extends — not collapses — creating the diagonal line from heel to fingertip. The lower hand or block provides light orientation, not structural weight-bearing.

Step 1: Begin in Virabhadrasana II
Start in Warrior II on the right side — front knee at 90 degrees over the front ankle, back leg straight, arms extended horizontally, gaze over the right hand.

Step 2: Extend the Right Side of the Body Over the Front Leg
Exhale and extend the right side of the body forward and down over the right thigh — the torso reaching laterally, not collapsing forward.

Step 3: Place the Front Hand
Bring the right hand to the floor beside the inner right foot, or rest the right forearm on the right thigh, or place the hand on a block. The front shoulder stays active — not collapsing into the joint.

Step 4: Extend the Back Arm Overhead
Extend the left arm overhead diagonally in the direction of the right foot — creating the single unbroken diagonal line from the outer left heel through the left hip, lateral ribcage, shoulder, and to the left fingertips.

Step 5: Rotate the Chest and Hold
Rotate the chest toward the ceiling — bottom and top ribs both reaching away from each other. Gaze upward toward the upper arm or forward. Hold 5–8 breaths with the front knee firmly over the front ankle.

Step 6: Rise and Switch Sides
Inhale and press through the back foot to rise back to Warrior II. Repeat on the left side.

Breathing in Utthita Parsvakonasana

Inhale to lengthen the diagonal line and lift the upper arm. Exhale to settle deeper without collapsing the front knee inward or the torso over the thigh. Steady Ujjayi breathing maintains the muscular engagement required for the sustained hold.

Preparatory Poses Before Utthita Parsvakonasana

Virabhadrasana II (Warrior II, 5 breaths each side) — Establishes the bent-knee leg position and hip opening before the lateral reach.

Trikonasana (Triangle Pose, 5 breaths each side) — Opens the lateral body with straight legs before the bent-knee version demands more sustained strength.

Prasarita Padottanasana (Wide-Legged Forward Fold, 5 breaths) — Warms the inner thighs and hamstrings in the wide stance.

Side body stretches in Tadasana (5 breaths each side) — Introduces the lateral lengthening pattern in a standing position.

Variations of Utthita Parsvakonasana

Variation 1: Utthita Parsvakonasana with Block — Beginner
Place a yoga block beside the front foot and rest the lower hand on it. The block raises the floor and allows the chest to rotate open fully and the diagonal line to form correctly — without the hip or shoulder flexibility that floor-hand placement requires.

Variation 2: Forearm on Thigh — Accessible
Rest the forearm on the front thigh rather than reaching the hand to the floor. This variation maintains the leg strength demand and the overhead arm extension while removing the lateral bend requirement — ideal for those with limited lateral flexibility or wrist sensitivity.

Variation 3: Baddha Parsvakonasana (Bound) — Advanced
The lower arm wraps under the front thigh and the upper arm reaches behind the back — hands clasping behind the back. The bound variation adds deep shoulder opening and thoracic rotation to the lateral strength of the base pose.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Utthita Parsvakonasana

Front knee collapsing inward — The front knee tracks the front toes throughout. Inward collapse places significant strain on the medial knee structures.

Torso collapsing over the front thigh — The torso extends laterally — not collapses forward. The diagonal line from heel to fingertip is the target shape; the torso lying on the thigh abandons the lateral extension entirely.

Back leg softening or bending — The back leg must remain fully extended and strongly engaged throughout — it is the structural anchor of the entire pose.

Chest facing the floor instead of rotating open — The chest rotates fully toward the ceiling. A downward-facing chest indicates insufficient thoracic rotation and collapses the lateral line.

Lower hand or forearm bearing the full body weight — The lower arm provides orientation — the pose is held by the legs and core, not the arm. Leaning into the lower arm removes the primary strengthening benefit.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Utthita Parsvakonasana?

Utthita Parsvakonasana is the Extended Side Angle Pose — a wide-legged standing pose in which the front knee is bent to 90 degrees, the lower arm reaches to the floor or block beside the front foot, and the upper arm extends overhead to create a single diagonal line from outer back heel to fingertip.

What are the utthita parsvakonasana benefits?

The primary utthita parsvakonasana benefits are: quadriceps and glute strengthening through the 90-degree front knee hold, maximum lateral body opening from heel to fingertip, abdominal organ stimulation, standing stamina building, full chest rotation and thoracic opening, and the development of the determined grounded effort the sustained pose demands.

How to do Utthita Parsvakonasana step by step?

Start in Warrior II. Exhale and extend the torso over the front leg. Place the front hand on the floor, block, or forearm on thigh. Extend the back arm overhead creating the diagonal line. Rotate the chest toward the ceiling. Hold 5–8 breaths with front knee over front ankle. Inhale to rise. Repeat on the left side.

What is the difference between Utthita Parsvakonasana and Trikonasana?

The key difference is the front knee: Trikonasana has both legs completely straight; Utthita Parsvakonasana has the front knee bent to 90 degrees. The bent knee makes Utthita Parsvakonasana more demanding for the quadriceps but more accessible for beginners (more floor clearance for the lower hand) and allows a deeper lateral torso extension.

How to modify Utthita Parsvakonasana for beginners?

Use a yoga block under the lower hand as the primary modification. The secondary modification is the forearm resting on the front thigh. Both modifications maintain the essential elements — leg strength demand and overhead arm extension — while removing the lateral flexibility requirement that beginners have not yet developed.

How long should I hold Utthita Parsvakonasana?

Hold for 5–8 breaths on each side — approximately 
30–45 seconds
. Build gradually to 10 breaths as strength and flexibility develop. The legs and core should remain strongly engaged throughout; if they fatigue significantly, reduce the hold time or use a modification.
