What is Kandharasana?

Kandharasana — pronounced kan-dah-RAH-sah-nah — derives from the Sanskrit kandha (shoulder or neck) and asana (pose). The English name is Shoulder Pose, reflecting the pose’s primary weight-bearing point on the shoulders and upper back in the supine bridge position. Kandharasana is closely related to Setu Bandha 
Sarvangasana
 (
Bridge Pose
) but is typically distinguished by the hand grip on the ankles — a modification that allows a deeper shoulder depression and more complete anterior chest opening than the free-arm bridge version.

Kandharasana — the Shoulder Pose, distinct from Setu Bandhasana though sharing a similar bridge position — is one of the most therapeutically prescribed supine backbends in the classical yoga tradition, specifically recommended for 
thyroid
 regulation, 
reproductive
 health and lower back strengthening. This complete guide covers kandharasana benefits, the precise technique that distinguishes it from standard Bridge Pose, and every modification for beginners through advanced practitioners.

Kandharasana belongs to the supine backbend category — one of the most accessible available categories for beginners because the floor supports much of the body weight, reducing the strength and 
flexibility
 requirements that standing backbends demand. The pose combines the hip extension and glute activation of Bridge Pose with the shoulder and chest opening produced by the arm position, and the anterior 
cervical
 stretch created by the neck’s gentle extension against the floor. This combination of effects makes kandharasana benefits particularly comprehensive for both structural health and energetic practice.

In the traditional yoga system, Kandharasana is valued for its direct stimulation of the thyroid and parathyroid glands through the combined shoulder and cervical region loading, its reproductive organ support through 
pelvic floor
 and hip extension, and its 
posterior chain
 strengthening. It appears in Bihar School of Yoga texts as one of the core therapeutic asanas for reproductive health, thyroid function and lower back strength — making its kandharasana benefits span a wider therapeutic range than its accessible appearance suggests.

How to Do Kandharasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Kandharasana should produce comfortable posterior chain activation and anterior chest opening — not lower back compression. If lower back pain occurs, reduce the hip lift height or revert to the free-arm Bridge Pose until posterior chain strength develops. The neck should remain in a natural, gentle extension — not forced or strained.

Step 1: Starting Position — Supine with Knees Bent

Lie on the back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart and approximately a forearm’s length from the buttocks. Arms rest alongside the body, palms facing down. Take 3 breaths here, feeling the natural weight of the back on the floor and the grounding of the feet.

Step 2: Reach for the Ankles

Keeping the back on the floor, reach the hands back to hold the ankles — wrapping the fingers around the outer ankle bones. This ankle grip is what distinguishes Kandharasana from standard Bridge Pose and produces the deeper shoulder depression. If ankles are not accessible, keep the arms alongside the body (standard Bridge) and approach ankle-hold as a progressive goal.

Step 3: Ground the Shoulders and Upper Arms

Before lifting, press the shoulders and upper arms firmly into the floor — feeling the shoulder blades draw toward the spine. This pre-activation of the shoulder girdle depression is the foundational action that gives Kandharasana its Shoulder Pose quality and protects the cervical spine during the lift.

Step 4: Inhale and Lift the Hips

On an inhalation, press through the feet and lift the hips toward the ceiling — engaging the gluteus maximus and hamstrings as the primary movers. The hips rise until the thighs are approximately parallel to the floor or slightly above. The ankle grip assists by drawing the chest open and the shoulders more deeply into the floor.

Step 5: Final Position and Hold

In the full Kandharasana, the hips are elevated, the chest is open, the shoulders are pressed firmly into the floor, the ankle grip maintains the shoulder depression, and the gaze is at the ceiling. Hold for 5-10 slow breaths with steady posterior chain engagement throughout. Feel the chest broadening with each inhalation.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Kandharasana

On an exhalation, slowly lower the hips one vertebra at a time — from the thoracic down to the sacrum — until the back is fully on the floor. Release the ankle grip and allow the arms to rest alongside the body. Rest in the supine position for 5 breaths before moving to the next pose or repeating.

Breathing in Kandharasana

Inhale to lift into Kandharasana — the breath expanding the chest as the pose creates the space for it. Breathe slowly and fully throughout the hold, feeling the ribs and sternum lift and broaden with each inhalation. Exhale to lower. The breath cycle and the hip movement are perfectly synchronised.

Preparatory Poses Before Kandharasana

Bitilasana-Marjaryasana (Cat-Cow) — warms the spinal extensors and establishes the breath-movement coordination before the more demanding Bridge lift.

Supta Baddha Konasana (Reclined Bound Angle) — opens the hips and inner thighs before the hip extension demand.

Shalabhasana (Locust Pose) — activates the gluteus maximus and posterior chain in preparation for the hip extension of Kandharasana.

Simple Bridge (free arms) — the preparatory version that develops the posterior chain strength before adding the ankle grip.

Variations of Kandharasana

Variation 1: Free-Arm Bridge (Beginner)

Standard Setu Bandha Sarvangasana with arms alongside the body — the preparatory version that develops posterior chain strength and spinal extension before the ankle-grip kandharasana depth is accessible. Difficulty: Beginner

Variation 2: Kandharasana with Interlaced Fingers (Intermediate)

Instead of ankle hold, interlace the fingers beneath the pelvis with arms extended along the floor — an intermediate shoulder-depression variation that creates more spinal extension than free-arm Bridge without requiring ankle flexibility. Difficulty: Intermediate

Variation 3: One-Leg Extended Kandharasana (Advanced)

From the full Kandharasana position, extend one leg toward the ceiling while maintaining the opposite foot on the floor — adding single-leg demand that maximises the posterior chain and core challenge. Difficulty: Advanced

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Kandharasana

Mistake 1: Knee Falling Outward or Inward
The knees should remain hip-width throughout Kandharasana — falling outward reduces glute activation and falling inward stresses the medial knee. Maintain the knee tracking over the second toe throughout the hold.

Mistake 2: Neck Strain from Forced Cervical Extension
The neck should remain in a natural, comfortable extension — not forced. If neck discomfort occurs, place a folded blanket under the upper back to reduce cervical extension demand.

Mistake 3: Using the Lower Back Instead of the Glutes to Maintain Height
If the 
glutes
 are insufficiently engaged, the lower back compensates by hyperextending — creating compression rather than the therapeutic extension Kandharasana intends. Maintain active glute contraction throughout the hold to ensure the posterior chain, not the lumbar spine, is the primary working structure.

Mistake 4: Gripping Toes Instead of Ankles
Gripping the toes rather than the ankles creates tibial rotation stress and reduces the shoulder-depression benefit of the grip. Hold the outer ankle firmly for the authentic Kandharasana shoulder opening.
