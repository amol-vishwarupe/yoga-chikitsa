Varada Mudra is a sacred gesture of giving, compassion, and the granting of boons — formed by extending the arm with the palm facing outward and downward, all fingers pointing toward the earth. Seen in depictions of Lakshmi, Buddha, and Tara, it cultivates genuine generosity, open-hearted compassion, and the Anahata chakra expansion that authentic giving embodies.

What is Varada Mudra?

Varada Mudra — the Boon-Granting Gesture — is one of the most universally recognisable sacred hand gestures in both Hindu and Buddhist iconography, appearing in the hands of thousands of divine figures across South and Southeast Asian sacred art. The gesture is formed by the extended arm, outward-facing palm, and earth-pointing fingers — the physical embodiment of the act of giving: the hand open, turned outward, offering downward from the heights of divine abundance to the needs of the world below.

In the Hindu tradition, Varada Mudra is most associated with Lakshmi, the goddess of abundance and grace — her right hand in Varada Mudra constantly pouring golden coins toward her devotees, representing the inexhaustible flow of divine generosity. In Buddhist practice, Varada Mudra is the gesture of compassionate giving offered by bodhisattvas as the physical symbol of their vow to give refuge and liberation to all beings.

Varada Mudra Benefits

Mental and Spiritual Benefits

Cultivates Genuine Generosity and Abundance Consciousness
The most direct benefit of consistent Varada Mudra practice is the cultivation of genuine generosity — the willingness to give from abundance rather than scarcity, to offer time, attention, care, and resources without the transactional quality that ego-based giving always carries. The regular embodiment of the giving gesture trains the neural pathways of generosity and abundance that determine the quality of all relationships.

Develops Compassion and Open-Hearted Presence
Varada Mudra’s boon-granting symbolism — the heart’s abundance flowing outward through the extended arm — directly cultivates the compassionate, open-hearted quality of presence that the gesture embodies. Sustained practice develops the genuine compassion and the sense of others’ wellbeing being as important as one’s own that advanced spiritual traditions consistently identify as the foundation of psychological health.

Activates the Anahata Chakra and Heart-Centred Awareness
The outward-flowing, giving gesture of Varada Mudra activates the Anahata (heart) chakra — the energy centre of love, compassion, and the expansive giving that open-heartedness enables. Regular practice progressively opens the cardiac plexus region and develops the quality of warm, expanded awareness associated with heart chakra activation.

Physical Benefits

Opens the Shoulder and Anterior Chest
The extended arm position of Varada Mudra gently opens the anterior shoulder capsule and the chest wall — counteracting the forward shoulder rounding that closed, self-protective postures produce. This physical opening complements the psychological opening of the gesture’s compassion cultivation.

How to Do Varada Mudra — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Key Principles The quality of intention is inseparable from Varada Mudra’s formation — the gesture held with the genuine intention of giving and compassion is fundamentally different from the same physical form held without intentional awareness. Cultivate the inner quality of generosity and openness alongside the physical formation.

Varada Mudra — Step by Step Step 1: Seated Starting Position
Sit in Sukhasana — spine erect, heart open, face relaxed. Take three full breaths to settle the body and establish the quality of open, giving awareness before forming the gesture.

Step 2: Extend the Right Arm
Extend the right arm forward and slightly outward — approximately 45 degrees from the body. The arm extends with ease, not force — the shoulder completely relaxed throughout.

Step 3: Turn the Palm Outward
Turn the palm outward — facing away from the body. All five fingers point gently toward the earth. The wrist and fingers are softly extended, not rigidly stiff.

Step 4: Rest the Left Hand in Mudra
Rest the left hand in Gyan Mudra (index finger and thumb touching) or Dhyana Mudra (hands in lap) on the left knee — the receptive and meditative complement to the giving gesture of the right hand.

Step 5: Breathe with Compassionate Intention
Breathe with heart-centred intention — each inhale drawing compassion and blessing into the heart; each exhale extending them outward through the giving arm. Hold for fifteen to thirty minutes.

Step 6: Release and Return
On a complete exhalation, gently lower the right arm to the lap. Rest in Gyan Mudra with both hands for two to three breaths before transitioning out of the practice.

Breathing in Varada Mudra

Heart-centred breathing accompanies Varada Mudra throughout — the inhalation drawing compassion and abundance into the heart, the exhalation extending these qualities outward through the giving arm. The breath rhythm is naturally slow and deep — four to six breaths per minute — creating the calm, generous quality that the gesture embodies.

Preparatory Practices Before Varada Mudra

These practices open the heart and cultivate the receptive awareness before Varada Mudra.

Matsyasana (Fish Pose, 5 minutes) — Opens the anterior chest and activates the Anahata chakra before the giving gesture.

Nadi Shodhana Pranayama (5 minutes) — Balances the solar and lunar channels to create the equilibrium from which genuine giving flows.

Loving-kindness (Metta) reflection — Silently generating the intention to give wellbeing to specific beings before forming the mudra.

Variations of Varada Mudra

Variation 1: Right Arm Only — Classical
The standard form described in the step-by-step instructions — right arm extended with giving palm, left hand in Gyan or Dhyana Mudra. This is the classical Varada Mudra formation as it appears in divine iconography, with the right hand giving and the left hand in meditative receptivity.

Variation 2: Both Arms in Varada Mudra — Full Giving
Both arms extended simultaneously in Varada Mudra — both palms outward, both sets of fingers pointing toward the earth. This bilateral form amplifies the giving quality and is used in practices specifically cultivating unconditional, boundless generosity.

Variation 3: Varada Mudra with Walking Meditation — Active Giving
Varada Mudra held during slow walking meditation — the giving gesture maintained while moving through space, transforming each step into an act of compassionate giving to the earth and all beings encountered. Practised in temples and sacred natural spaces.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Varada Mudra

Holding the Arm with Muscular Tension
The extended arm of Varada Mudra must be held with ease, not muscular effort — the gesture of giving is effortless and abundant, not strained and depleting. Allow the arm to be supported by the skeleton rather than the muscles, the shoulder completely dropped away from the ear throughout the hold.

Practising Without Genuine Intention
Varada Mudra held as a physical form without the cultivated intention of generosity and compassion is the hand position alone — not the complete mudra. The gesture’s transformative power comes from the simultaneous cultivation of the inner quality that the outer form represents.

Neglecting the Left Hand and Receptive Balance
Varada Mudra’s giving quality requires the complementary receptivity of the left hand — the balance between giving (right) and receiving (left) that prevents the depletion of one-sided giving. Always maintain the left hand in a meditative mudra throughout the practice.

Who Should Practise Varada Mudra?

Those Cultivating Generosity and Heart-Centred Living
Varada Mudra is the most direct embodied practice for cultivating genuine generosity — particularly valuable for those whose natural constitution tends toward holding, scarcity thinking, or the self-protective contraction that prevents authentic giving.

Meditators Developing Compassion and Metta Practice
For practitioners engaged in formal loving-kindness (Metta) or compassion meditation, Varada Mudra provides the physical gesture that anchors and amplifies the inner cultivation — the body’s giving posture reinforcing the mind’s compassionate intention.

Is Varada Mudra Good for Beginners?
Yes — the physical formation is simple and immediately accessible. The depth of the practice develops with the quality of intention cultivated over weeks and months of consistent use.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is Varada Mudra correctly formed and does arm position matter?

The arm extends downward with the palm facing outward and forward, fingers pointing toward the earth — all five fingers are held together naturally, neither rigidly spread nor tightly closed. The arm position is significant: the downward extension is not casual but intentional, as it channels energy from the heart downward through the palm in an active gesture of giving, distinct from mudras where hands rest in the lap passively.

In which iconographic depictions is Varada Mudra most prominently seen and what does its visual presence communicate?

It appears on statues and paintings of Avalokiteshvara, Tara, Lakshmi, and numerous Bodhisattvas across Buddhist and Hindu traditions — almost universally in the left hand while the right holds another mudra. Its presence in deity iconography communicates that the figure is in an active state of bestowing grace, boons, and compassion upon devotees, making it one of the most devotionally significant hand gestures in South and Southeast Asian sacred art.

Can Varada Mudra be practiced during seated meditation and how does posture affect its energetic quality?

Yes — it is practiced seated with the arm resting along the left thigh, palm turned outward, and is often paired with Abhaya Mudra in the right hand to create the combined gesture of fearlessness and giving simultaneously. Sitting upright with an open chest is essential, as a collapsed posture physically and energetically restricts the heart-to-hand flow that defines this mudra’s essence — the openness of the chest directly mirrors and supports the openness of the giving gesture.

Which chakra is most activated through Varada Mudra and how does the practice cultivate that quality internally?

The Anahata (Heart) Chakra is the primary energetic center engaged — the downward-flowing open palm is understood as an external expression of Anahata energy radiating outward and downward into the world. Internally, sustained practice is said to dissolve the hoarding tendencies of a contracted heart, gradually replacing fear-based withholding with an embodied sense of abundance from which giving flows naturally rather than effortfully.

Does Varada Mudra have documented effects on psychological states related to generosity and giving?

Within somatic psychology and embodied cognition research, adopting open, downward-facing palm postures has been associated with reduced defensive arousal, increased feelings of social warmth, and greater activation of prosocial motivation. Yogic tradition corroborates this from a different angle — describing how the body posture of giving, when held consistently, gradually reshapes the practitioner’s habitual psychological orientation from contraction toward openness, making generosity a felt body-state rather than merely a cognitive value.

What mantra or visualization practice traditionally accompanies Varada Mudra for deeper effect?

In Tibetan Buddhist practice, Varada Mudra is paired with visualizations of golden nectar flowing from the open palm to all suffering beings — accompanied by mantras of compassion such as Om Mani Padme Hum. In Hindu devotional practice, chanting Om Shreem Hreem Kleem Namah or Lakshmi-related stotras while holding the mudra is traditional, connecting the gesture of giving with the archetype of divine abundance from which all true giving originates.

How does practicing Varada Mudra influence one’s relationship with material and emotional attachment over time?

Long-term practitioners describe a gradual loosening of the psychological grip around resources — whether material wealth, emotional energy, time, or knowledge. The mudra acts as a daily embodied rehearsal of release, and traditional texts suggest that consistent practice over months rewires the subtle body’s default orientation from accumulation to circulation — reflecting the yogic understanding that prana, like water, becomes stagnant when hoarded and vital when allowed to flow freely through and beyond the self.
