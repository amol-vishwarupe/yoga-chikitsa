What is Vrschikasana?

Vrschikasana  pronounced vrish-chi-kah-sa-na  translates as “Scorpion Pose.” The Sanskrit “vrschika” means scorpion, and the pose is named for its visual resemblance: the body in forearm balance with the feet curled forward toward the head, like a scorpion’s curled tail.

Scorpion pose is one of the most advanced postures in modern yoga, combining the strength and balance of pincha mayurasana (forearm balance) with the deep backbend of urdhva dhanurasana (wheel pose). It appears in advanced ashtanga and intermediate-to-advanced hatha sequences, and is considered an aspirational pose for many committed practitioners.

The half scorpion pose is a more accessible (but still advanced) version where the legs remain straighter rather than curling fully toward the head. Even half scorpion requires established forearm balance and significant backbend mobility.

Vrschikasana Benefits

Physical Benefits

Builds Extreme Shoulder, Arm, and Core Strength
Scorpion pose benefits include the most demanding upper-body and core strengthening in the asana family. The forearm balance alone requires serious shoulder strength.

Improves Spinal Mobility and Backbend Capacity
The deep upper-back extension required for scorpion develops spinal mobility beyond what most other poses can produce.

Stretches the Front Body Deeply
The combination of inversion and backbend stretches the chest, abdomen, and hip flexors in ways no other single pose does.

Builds Balance and Body Awareness
Holding inverted balance with a curving spine demands precise body awareness  this skill carries into every other pose.

Builds Mental Resilience and Fearlessness
Approaching scorpion pose develops mental discipline. The pose demands consistency, patience, and the willingness to face fear safely.

Energising and Empowering
Like all inversions, scorpion is highly energising. The deep backbend amplifies this effect.

How to Do Vrschikasana Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Do not attempt this pose without years of foundation. Pincha mayurasana (forearm balance) must be solid first. Wheel pose must be accessible. Even then, work toward scorpion under qualified instruction with adequate spotting.

Step 1: Starting Position

Begin in dolphin pose with forearms parallel, shoulder-width apart.

Step 2: Establish Pincha Mayurasana

Kick up into a vertical forearm balance. Hold for several breaths to establish stability.

Step 3: Begin the Backbend

Slowly engage the upper back into extension, opening the chest. The head lifts to look forward.

Step 4: Bend the Knees

Once the upper back has opened, bend the knees and allow the feet to drop toward the head  slowly and with control.

Step 5: Final Position and Hold

The feet curl toward the head. Hold for 3–5 breaths only. Maintain awareness of the cervical spine.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Vrschikasana

Slowly straighten the legs back to vertical. Exit pincha mayurasana by lowering one leg at a time. Rest in child’s pose for several breaths to release the spine.

Breathing in Vrschikasana

Slow, controlled. Holding the breath in this position is dangerous. If you can’t breathe normally, the pose is too deep to exit immediately.

Preparatory Poses Before Vrschikasana

Pincha Mayurasana (Forearm Balance)  The non-negotiable foundation.

Urdhva Dhanurasana (Wheel Pose)  Builds the backbend capacity.

Bhujangasana (Cobra)  Develops gentle backbend tolerance.

Ardha Pincha Mayurasana (Dolphin)  Builds shoulder strength.

Variations of Vrschikasana

Variation 1: Half Scorpion Pose (Intermediate)

Maintain forearm balance with knees bent at 90 degrees. The feet hover in space rather than curling toward the head. Significantly safer and more accessible.

Variation 2: Wall-Supported Scorpion (Practice Safety)

Practise the pose with the wall behind you so the feet can rest against it for support. Build the shape safely.

Variation 3: Full Scorpion (Advanced Qualified Instruction Only)

The complete pose with the feet touching the head. Years of dedicated work required. Only practise under qualified, in-person guidance.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Vrschikasana

Mistake 1: Attempting without forearm balance mastery. Trying scorpion without solid pincha mayurasana causes falls. Correction: Master forearm balance for at least 6 months before approaching scorpion.

Mistake 2: Forcing the backbend. Correction: Build wheel pose mobility separately first.

Mistake 3: Compressing the cervical spine. Correction: Lift the head actively rather than letting it drop back.

Mistake 4: Holding too long. Correction: 3–5 breaths maximum, even at advanced level.

Mistake 5: Practising without instruction or spotting. Correction: Always under qualified, in-person guidance  never alone.

Who Should Practice Vrschikasana?

Advanced Yoga Practitioners
Years of consistent practice required. Pincha mayurasana and wheel pose must be solid first.

Those with Established Inversion and Backbend Practice
Both prerequisites are essential.

Is Half Scorpion Pose Good for Beginners?
No. Even the half version requires established forearm balance  typically 1–2 years of practice. Beginners should focus on building toward dolphin pose, then forearm balance, before considering any scorpion variation. Start with our

Frequently Asked Questions about Vrschikasana

What is Vrschikasana?

Scorpion poses  an advanced inverted backbend on the forearms with the feet curling toward the head. One of the most demanding poses in yoga.

Is Half Scorpion Pose for Beginners?

No. Even the half version requires established forearm balance  typically 1–2 years of consistent practice.

What Are the Benefits of Scorpion Pose?

Extreme shoulder, arm, and core strength; deep backbend capacity; balance and body awareness; mental resilience. The benefits compound over years of patient work.

How Long Does it Take to Learn Vrschikasana?

Years. Most practitioners need 2–4 years of consistent foundational work before safely approaching even half a scorpion.

Can I Practise Scorpion Pose at Home Alone?

No. Always under qualified, in-person guidance with proper spotting. The cervical-spine risk is too high for self-practice.
