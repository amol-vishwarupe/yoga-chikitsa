Supta Konasana, or Reclining Angle Pose, opens the inner thighs through gravity-assisted inversion from Sarvangasana (Shoulder Stand) while maintaining thyroid stimulation and deep parasympathetic activation. It uniquely stretches the adductors in a decompressed inverted position that no seated or standing variation can replicate. Suitable for intermediate practitioners as part of the complete Sarvangasana inversion sequence.

What is Supta Konasana?

Supta Konasana — known in English as Reclining Angle Pose or Inverted Wide-Angle Pose — derives from Sanskrit: Supta (reclining or supine), Kona (angle), and Asana (posture). From Sarvangasana, the legs open wide to the sides in a wide V-shape above the head — gravity providing a unique passive adductor stretch that no floor-based variation can replicate.

Supta Konasana combines the thyroid-stimulating and parasympathetic-activating benefits of the shoulder stand inversion with the adductor and inner thigh stretching of wide-angle postures — but from an inverted position where gravity provides the traction. This gravity-assisted stretch produces a different quality of adductor release — the tissue lengthens in a decompressed, supported position that reduces the protective muscular guarding that floor-based stretching often triggers.

Supta Konasana Benefits

Physical Benefits

Opens the Inner Thighs Through Gravity-Assisted Inversion Stretch
The inverted wide-leg position allows gravity to gently traction the adductors without the protective muscular guarding that floor-based stretching produces. This gravity-led, decompressed inner thigh opening develops adductor flexibility from a unique angle — the downward gravitational pull on the spread legs creating traction that no seated or standing stretch replicates.

Stimulates the Thyroid and Endocrine System
Entering from Sarvangasana, Supta Konasana maintains the cervical extension and throat compression that directly stimulate the thyroid and parathyroid glands — providing dual endocrine and adductor benefits within the same inversion hold.

Improves Venous Return and Supports Cardiovascular Health
The maintained inversion continues the improved venous return and cardiac load reduction of the shoulder stand — blood pooled in the lower limbs during upright activity draining toward the heart, contributing to the blood pressure regulatory effects of the inversion family.

Relieves Pelvic Congestion and Supports Menstrual Health
The inverted wide-leg position provides unique relief from pelvic congestion — the reversal of gravitational blood flow combined with bilateral hip opening reducing the pelvic tension associated with menstrual discomfort.

Mental and Emotional Benefits

Deepens Parasympathetic Activation and Supports Sleep Quality
Continuing from the shoulder stand, Supta Konasana deepens the parasympathetic activation — the combined inversion and wide-angle release produce a quality of deep physical and nervous system rest that makes the complete Sarvangasana-Supta Konasana sequence one of yoga’s most reliable tools for insomnia management and evening stress recovery.

Produces a Distinctive Inversion-Aided Sense of Release
The wide-leg spread in the inverted position creates a distinctive physical and mental experience — a sense of expansion and spaciousness that differs qualitatively from both standing and seated hip opening. The unique gravity-tracted opening is consistently reported as one of the most pleasurable sensations in the complete yoga practice.

How to Do Supta Konasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Key Principles Two foundational principles: establish a stable Sarvangasana first — the shoulder support, spinal alignment, and neck safety of the shoulder stand must be thoroughly established before the leg spread; and open the legs slowly — moving too quickly in the inverted position can cause acute adductor strain due to gravity-assisted loading.

Supta Konasana — Step by Step Step 1: Establish Sarvangasana (Shoulder Stand)
Begin in a stable, aligned Sarvangasana — both legs fully extended upward, hands supporting the mid-back with fingers pointing upward, weight evenly distributed across both shoulders. The neck must be comfortable and neutral. Establish steady breathing before proceeding.

Step 2: Fix the Gaze and Stabilise the Head
Fix the gaze upward or toward the navel. Ensure the head is completely still and central — no rotation in any direction. The neck position in shoulder stand is the primary safety factor for all its variations.

Step 3: Slowly Open Both Legs Wide
On the exhale, slowly begin opening both legs apart — spreading them wide in a V-shape above the head. Allow gravity to assist the opening rather than muscularly forcing the spread.

Step 4: Spread to the Comfortable Edge
Continue opening to the comfortable edge — a sustained, manageable inner thigh stretch. There is no target angle. The sustainable edge where the breath remains easy is the correct position.

Step 5: Optional Toe Hold
If flexibility allows, reach the arms to hold the big toes or inner arches — maintaining the wide-leg spread while the back support moves to the hands-to-feet connection. If not yet accessible, keep the hands on the mid-back.

Step 6: Hold, Return to Sarvangasana, Then Roll Down
Hold for five to fifteen breath cycles, breathing into the inner thighs. To exit: inhale and bring the legs together, returning to Sarvangasana. Roll down slowly to the floor — vertebra by vertebra.

Breathing in Supta Konasana

Breathing inverted is distinctive — the diaphragm works against gravity. Direct the breath into the inner thighs and lower abdomen — each inhale creates a gentle internal expansion that softens the adductors; each exhale allows the legs to drift fractionally wider. Never allow breath-holding, which increases adductor guarding and resists the gravity-assisted opening.

Preparatory Poses Before Supta Konasana

These poses warm the adductors and establish the inversion base before Supta Konasana.

Baddha Konasana (3 minutes) — Warms the hip external rotators and inner thighs in the most accessible seated position.

Upavistha Konasana (2 minutes) — Directly warms the adductors in the seated wide-angle position before the inverted version.

Sarvangasana (stable hold, 1-3 minutes) — The essential inversion that Supta Konasana is entered from — must be stable and comfortable before proceeding.

Variations of Supta Konasana

Variation 1: Standard Supta Konasana — From Sarvangasana
Both legs opened wide from full Shoulder Stand with hands remaining on the mid-back for support — the primary form for all practitioners beginning Supta Konasana practice.

Variation 2: Supta Konasana with Toe Hold — Advanced
The hands release from the mid-back to clasp the big toes — maintaining the wide-leg spread while the arms are free. Requires sufficient adductor flexibility to hold the inverted position without the back-supporting hand base.

Variation 3: Viparita Karani Wide-Leg — Accessible Alternative
From legs-up-the-wall (Viparita Karani), opening the legs wide — a fully supported, lower-intensity alternative providing similar adductor and pelvic benefits without the shoulder stand’s cervical requirements. The recommended starting point for those not yet in full Sarvangasana.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Supta Konasana

Turning the Head During the Inversion
The most consequential and potentially injurious error in all shoulder stand variations. The neck is under load in the cervical extension position — any rotation while inverted concentrates rotational stress on the cervical discs and facet joints. The head remains completely still and central throughout the entire inversion.

Opening the Legs Too Quickly
The gravity-assisted leg spread can produce acute adductor strain if the opening is rapid — gravity amplifies the opening force beyond what the adductors can safely absorb in a quick movement. Always move the legs apart slowly — spending two to three breath cycles opening rather than dropping to the edge immediately.

Attempting Supta Konasana Before a Stable Sarvangasana Is Established
Supta Konasana’s safety depends entirely on the stability of the Sarvangasana base. A shoulder stand that is unstable, uncomfortable, or not properly aligned makes the wide-leg variation unsafe.

Who Should Practise Supta Konasana?

Practitioners Who Have Established a Stable Sarvangasana
Supta Konasana is the natural next variation within the Sarvangasana family — for all practitioners who have established a comfortable, aligned Shoulder Stand and are progressing through the complete inversion sequence.

Those Seeking Gravity-Assisted Adductor Opening
The gravity-assisted inversion stretch provides a quality of adductor release that is difficult to replicate through seated or standing stretches — a valuable complement for practitioners working toward deep hip openness in Upavistha Konasana and related postures.

Is Supta Konasana Good for Beginners?
Not as a starting practice — the Viparita Karani wide-leg variation is the appropriate beginner-accessible alternative. Supta Konasana requires an established, stable Sarvangasana as a prerequisite. Beginners progress through Setu Bandhasana and Viparita Karani before the full inversion sequence.

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes Supta Konasana different from seated Upavistha Konasana?

In Upavistha Konasana, gravity pulls the legs toward the floor as the adductors resist. In Supta Konasana, the body is inverted — gravity pulls the legs downward from the overhead inversion position, creating a traction on the adductors in a completely decompressed, gravity-assisted direction that no seated stretch can replicate. This different gravitational direction produces a qualitatively different quality of adductor release.

Why must I open my legs slowly in Supta Konasana?

The gravity-assisted leg spread can produce acute adductor strain if the opening is rapid — gravity amplifies the opening force beyond what the adductors can safely absorb in a quick movement. Always move the legs apart slowly, spending two to three breath cycles opening rather than dropping to the edge immediately. The same principle applies to closing the legs — bring them back together slowly on an inhalation.

Who should avoid Supta Konasana?

Anyone who should avoid Sarvangasana — those with cervical spine injuries, uncontrolled high blood pressure, glaucoma, detached retina, acute ear infections, or pregnancy beyond the first trimester — should also avoid Supta Konasana as it enters from Shoulder Stand. Viparita Karani Wide-Leg (legs up the wall with knees open) is the accessible alternative for those avoiding inversions.

What is the Viparita Karani Wide-Leg alternative to Supta Konasana?

Viparita Karani is performed with the hips supported by a bolster close to a wall and the legs extending upward. Opening the legs wide against the wall provides gravity-assisted adductor stretching without the cervical compression or shoulder-bearing demands of Sarvangasana. This is the appropriate starting practice for those not yet in a stable Sarvangasana.

Can I hold the toes in Supta Konasana?

Yes — if adductor flexibility allows, reaching the arms to hold the big toes or inner arches creates a self-supported wide-leg shape in the inversion with the hands free from the mid-back. This requires sufficient adductor flexibility to hold the inverted wide-leg position without the back support of the hands. Begin with hands remaining on the mid-back and progress to the toe hold as flexibility develops.

What should I do after Supta Konasana before rolling down?

Inhale and bring the legs together — returning to full Sarvangasana — before rolling down. Never roll down from the wide-leg position. The return to Sarvangasana re-stabilises the shoulder base before the controlled vertebra-by-vertebra descent to the floor.

How does Supta Konasana benefit menstrual health?

The inverted wide-leg position provides unique relief from pelvic congestion — the reversal of gravitational blood flow combined with bilateral hip opening reducing the pelvic tension associated with menstrual discomfort. However, the traditional yoga recommendation is to avoid inversions including Supta Konasana during heavy flow days of menstruation. It is most beneficial as a regular practice between menstrual cycles.
