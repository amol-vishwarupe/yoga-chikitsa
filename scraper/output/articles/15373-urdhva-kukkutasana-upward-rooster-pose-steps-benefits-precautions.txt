What is Urdhva Kukkutasana?

Urdhva Kukkutasana derives from three Sanskrit words: ‘urdhva’ meaning upward, ‘kukkuta’ meaning rooster, and ‘asana’ meaning pose — together translating to “the upward rooster pose.” Pronounced oord-vah kook-koo-TAH-suh-nuh, the pose is also known in English as Upward Rooster Pose. Visually, the practitioner combines the lotus leg position with an arm balance — typically entering from a headstand (Sirsasana), bringing the legs into Padmasana (lotus), and then transitioning into a high arm balance with the lotus legs lifted high above the arms. It is one of the most demanding combination poses in classical yoga, requiring substantial hip flexibility, core strength, and shoulder stability simultaneously.

The pose is named for the rooster — a symbol of dawn, vigilance, and elevated awareness in Indian spiritual tradition. The “urdhva” (upward) prefix distinguishes this advanced version from standard Kukkutasana, which is performed seated on the floor with the arms threaded through the lotus legs. Documented in classical Hatha yoga texts and prominently featured in B.K.S. Iyengar’s Light on Yoga, Urdhva Kukkutasana represents the integration of inversion, arm balance, and seated meditation pose into a single complex posture. The traditional symbolism connects to the rooster’s elevated perch — the practitioner rises above the ground through arm strength while maintaining the meditative lotus seat.

In the broader yoga system, Urdhva Kukkutasana belongs to the family of advanced arm balances and inversions — alongside Bakasana (Crane Pose), Astavakrasana, and other compound poses that combine multiple foundational elements. It is most commonly practised within advanced Ashtanga sequences, advanced Iyengar progressions, and as a peak pose in intermediate-to-advanced yoga classes. Most teachers reserve Urdhva Kukkutasana for practitioners with established Padmasana, Sirsasana, and Bakasana foundations — typically requiring years of consistent practice before this pose becomes safely accessible.

How to Do Urdhva Kukkutasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Three foundational principles must be in place: established Sirsasana (headstand) hold of 3+ minutes, comfortable Padmasana (lotus) hold of 5+ minutes, and established Bakasana (crane) hold of 30+ seconds. Without these foundations, Urdhva Kukkutasana is not safe to attempt. Use a wall and qualified instructor for early attempts.

Step 1: Starting Position (Sirsasana)

Begin in Sirsasana (Headstand) with body fully inverted, legs extended toward the ceiling, and core firmly engaged. Establish stability with three steady breaths.

Step 2: Bring Legs into Padmasana

Slowly and carefully bring the legs into Padmasana (Lotus Pose) while remaining in headstand. Cross the right foot onto the left thigh and the left foot onto the right thigh. The lotus leg position must be established before the next phase.

Step 3: Place Hands Firmly on the Mat Beside the Head

Place both hands flat on the mat with fingers spread wide, positioned slightly forward of the head. The hands must be set firmly because they will bear the entire body weight in the next step.

Step 4: Engage Core and Begin to Lift off the Head

Engage the core powerfully and begin to press the hands into the mat to lift the head off the floor. The body weight transfers from the head to the hands. The lotus legs lift higher into the air.

Step 5: Final Position and Hold

In the final position, the entire body weight rests on the hands, the lotus legs are lifted high above the arms, and the head is suspended in space. Hold for 5–10 breaths initially, building to 
30 seconds
 over months of consistent practice.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Urdhva Kukkutasana

To exit, slowly lower the head back to the mat, returning to Sirsasana with legs in Padmasana. Then gently release the lotus and return to standard Sirsasana, before lowering the legs and resting in Balasana (Child’s Pose). Always exit with control.

Breathing in Urdhva Kukkutasana

The breath in Urdhva Kukkutasana follows a steady deep pattern despite the intensity of the pose — inhale to lift, exhale to settle into the hold, continue with steady breathing throughout. Holding the breath in such an inverted load increases blood pressure dangerously; smooth breathing is essential. If breathing becomes ragged, exit immediately.

Preparatory Poses Before Urdhva Kukkutasana

Preparation requires years of foundational asana practice — these prerequisites must be established before the pose is attempted.

Sirsasana (Headstand)

Must be held comfortably for 3+ minutes — the inverted foundation of Urdhva Kukkutasana.

Padmasana (Lotus Pose)

Must be held comfortably for 5+ minutes — the leg position of Urdhva Kukkutasana.

Bakasana (Crane Pose)

Must be held comfortably for 30+ seconds — the arm balance foundation that transfers into Urdhva Kukkutasana.

Kukkutasana (Standard Rooster Pose)

The seated lotus arm balance — the foundational version of the pose, building the arm and core strength required for the inverted version.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Urdhva Kukkutasana

Mistake 1: Attempting Without Established Foundations
The most dangerous mistake. Attempting Urdhva Kukkutasana without comfortable Sirsasana, Padmasana, and Bakasana foundations risks acute neck, shoulder, or wrist injury. Correction: build all three foundational poses to comfortable hold capacity before attempting Urdhva Kukkutasana — this typically requires years of consistent practice.

Mistake 2: Forcing the Lotus Leg Position
Practitioners who cannot comfortably enter Padmasana often force the leg position to attempt Urdhva Kukkutasana — risking acute knee injury. Correction: build Padmasana hip mobility separately for months before combining with arm balance attempts.

Mistake 3: Holding the Breath During Transition
The intensity of the transition makes breath-holding common — but breath-holding spikes blood pressure dangerously in inverted positions. Correction: prioritise smooth steady breathing throughout the pose; if breath becomes ragged, exit immediately.

Mistake 4: Practising Without a Wall or Spotter Initially
Solo unsupported practice on first attempts risks falling and injury. Correction: practise with a wall behind the body for the first 20+ attempts, and ideally with a qualified instructor present for hands-on guidance.

Mistake 5: Skipping the Counter-Pose
The intense compression of the wrists, shoulders, and core requires recovery — without counter-poses, the body remains in stress patterns. Correction: always follow with Balasana (Child’s Pose), wrist circles, and gentle backbends to restore neutral alignment.

Frequently Asked Questions about Urdhva Kukkutasana

What is Urdhva Kukkutasana?

Urdhva Kukkutasana is the Sanskrit name for the Upward Rooster Pose — an advanced combination yoga pose where the practitioner enters a headstand, brings the legs into lotus position, and transitions into an arm balance with the lotus legs lifted above the arms.

Is Urdhva Kukkutasana Good for Beginners?

No — Urdhva Kukkutasana is an advanced pose requiring established headstand, lotus, and crane pose foundations. Beginners should focus on foundational poses for years before attempting this combination.

What is the Difference between Urdhva Kukkutasana and Kukkutasana?

Standard Kukkutasana is performed seated with the legs in lotus position and the arms threaded through the lotus to lift the body off the floor. Urdhva Kukkutasana adds the inversion — the practitioner inverts via headstand and lifts into the arm balance.

Can Urdhva Kukkutasana Help with Weight Loss?

The pose is not a weight loss practice — it is an advanced strength and balance pose. As part of a comprehensive yoga practice including dynamic flow, the broader practice supports weight management.

How Many Calories Does Urdhva Kukkutasana Burn?

A 30-second hold burns minimal calories directly — perhaps 5–10. Its value is in strength, balance, and mental focus development, not caloric expenditure.

How Often Should I Practice Urdhva Kukkutasana?

For practitioners with established foundations, 
2–3 times
 per week is appropriate — the wrist, shoulder, and neck loading requires recovery between sessions. Daily attempts risk overuse injury.

What Should I Wear for Urdhva Kukkutasana Practice?

Wear fitted, stretchy clothing that does not slide during inversions — yoga leggings or fitted shorts and a fitted top work best. Avoid loose tops that fall over the face during the inverted position.

Can I Do Urdhva Kukkutasana at Home Online?

Only with established prerequisite poses and ideally with live guidance for early form correction. Live online sessions help assess readiness and prevent the injury that solo attempts often produce in advanced poses.
