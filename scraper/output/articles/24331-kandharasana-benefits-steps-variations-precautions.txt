Kandharasana (Shoulder Pose): Steps, Benefits & Precautions

What is Kandharasana?

Kandharasana comes from the Sanskrit word 
Kandha
, meaning shoulder, and 
asana
, meaning posture. In English, it is widely known as the 
Shoulder Pose
 — pronounced kahn-dha-RAH-suh-nah. The pose is performed lying on your back with your feet flat on the floor, knees bent, and hips lifted while the shoulders remain firmly pressed into the ground. The body forms a graceful arch from shoulders to knees, supported by the strength of the lower back, glutes, and thighs.

In traditional yogic texts, Kandharasana is associated with the Satyananda tradition and is one of the foundational supine backbends prescribed before more demanding inversions. It stimulates the spinal column, the reproductive organs, and the thyroid — making it one of the most therapeutically valued poses for both men and women. Unlike advanced backbends that require significant shoulder or wrist flexibility, Kandharasana is approachable for most practitioners while still offering meaningful physiological benefits.

Within the broader yoga system, this pose occupies a unique position: it bridges restorative practice and active strengthening. It is close in shape to 
Setu Bandhasana
 (Bridge Pose) but differs in the placement of the arms — in Kandharasana, the arms remain alongside the body or clasp the ankles, creating a stronger contraction in the posterior chain. Whether you are building a morning routine or recovering from long hours of sitting, Kandharasana is a pose worth making central to your practice.

Kandharasana Benefits

The 
kandharasana benefits
 span the physical, hormonal, and emotional dimensions of health. Here is a detailed breakdown across both categories.

Physical Benefits

Benefit 1: Strengthens the Spine and Lower Back Muscles
When you lift your hips in Kandharasana, the erector spinae and multifidus muscles along your spine engage deeply to hold the arch. Regular practice gradually builds spinal endurance and reduces the muscular fatigue that leads to chronic lower back discomfort. For people who sit at a desk for hours, this is one of the most practical poses that supports 
back pain management through yoga
.

Benefit 2: Improves Flexibility in the Hips, Hamstrings, and Thoracic Spine
Holding the Shoulder Pose lengthens the hip flexors — particularly the psoas and iliacus — which tend to become shortened and tight with prolonged sitting. The thoracic spine is gently mobilised with each repetition, contributing to improved posture over time. Pairing this pose with 
targeted yoga for flexibility
 can accelerate these gains meaningfully.

Benefit 3: Stimulates the Thyroid, Reproductive Organs, and Digestive System
The arched position of the body in Kandharasana creates gentle compression and then release in the abdominal region, stimulating peristalsis and supporting healthy digestion. The pose also increases blood flow to the pelvic region, which is known to support the functioning of reproductive organs. In women, regular practice may help ease menstrual discomfort and pelvic tension when practised consistently over several weeks.

Benefit 4: Opens the Chest and Supports Respiratory Health
As the hips lift and the shoulders press down, the chest naturally opens and expands. This gentle thoracic extension creates more space for the lungs to fill completely, improving breathing capacity over time. People who experience tightness across the upper chest — often linked to stress or a hunched posture — tend to notice a gradual sense of ease in breathing after consistent Kandharasana practice.

Benefit 5: Calms the Nervous System and Supports Stress Management
The rhythmic lift and release of the hips in Kandharasana, combined with slow diaphragmatic breathing, activates the parasympathetic nervous system. This helps the body shift out of a stress response and into a state of calm alertness. Practitioners who include this pose in a daily morning routine often report feeling more grounded and less reactive throughout the day — a quality that deepens with consistent practice, much like the broader approach explored in 
yoga for stress management
.

Benefit 6: Improves Focus, Hormonal Balance, and Mood
Kandharasana’s effect on the endocrine system — particularly the thyroid and pituitary glands — means that regular practice may gradually support hormonal regulation. Stable hormones contribute to steadier moods, more consistent energy levels, and sharper focus across the day. For those navigating mood fluctuations tied to hormonal shifts, this pose is a gentle, non-invasive addition to a supportive routine.

Mental and Emotional Benefits

How to Do Kandharasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Before beginning, keep three principles in mind. First, never force the lift — let your glutes and lower back do the work, not momentum. Second, keep your feet parallel and hip-width apart throughout the pose. Third, breathe steadily; the lift happens on the inhale, and you hold or release on the exhale. Rushing through this pose reduces its benefit significantly.

Step 1: Starting Position

Lie flat on your back on a yoga mat. Bend both knees and bring your feet flat onto the floor, placing them hip-width apart and close to your sitting bones — roughly 30–40 cm from your hips. Let your arms rest alongside your body with palms facing downward. Soften your jaw, relax your shoulders away from your ears, and take two or three natural breaths to settle in.

Step 2: Grounding the Shoulders and Feet

Press your feet firmly and evenly into the mat — feel the four corners of each foot grounded. At the same time, draw your shoulder blades gently toward each other underneath your back. This creates a stable foundation and prevents the lower back from over-arching during the lift. You should feel a mild engagement in your upper back and glutes even before you begin to rise.

Step 3: Lifting the Hips

On a slow inhale, press through your feet and lift your hips toward the ceiling. Rise vertebra by vertebra — lower back first, then mid-back, then upper back — until your body forms a straight diagonal line from your shoulders to your knees. Do not let your knees splay outward; keep them tracking directly over your middle toes. The lift should feel like a lengthening, not a compression.

Step 4: Arm and Hand Position

In the classical version of Kandharasana, reach your hands back toward your ankles and clasp them gently. If that is not accessible today, simply press your palms firmly into the mat alongside your hips. Clasping the ankles draws the shoulders further underneath the body, deepens the chest opening, and increases the contraction in the posterior chain. Choose the variation that allows you to breathe comfortably.

Step 5: Final Position and Hold

Once in the lifted position, hold the pose for 
30 seconds
 to 
1 minute
, breathing slowly and evenly. Keep your thighs parallel and your weight distributed evenly across both shoulders. Feel the chest broad and open, the glutes gently engaged, and the lower back long rather than compressed. If you sense pinching in the lower back, reduce the height of your lift slightly until the sensation eases.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Kandharasana

On an exhale, slowly lower your spine back to the mat — upper back first, then mid-back, then lower back and hips. Do not drop down; control the descent vertebra by vertebra. Once flat, bring your knees together over your chest and rock gently side to side for a moment to release the lower back. This counter-movement prevents stiffness and helps the spine reset after the backbend.

Breathing in Kandharasana

Inhale as you lift the hips. Hold the breath briefly at the top if comfortable, or continue slow, natural breaths while in the final position. Exhale completely as you lower back down. The breath is not incidental — it is what makes the movement therapeutic. Shallow or held breathing during the hold reduces the nervous-system benefits of the pose considerably.

Preparatory Poses Before Kandharasana

A brief warm-up makes Kandharasana safer and more effective. These four poses prepare the exact muscle groups the pose demands.

Supta Baddha Konasana (Reclined Butterfly):
 Opens the inner hips and groin, reducing resistance when the hips lift.

Pawanmuktasana (Wind-Relieving Pose):
 Releases lower back tension and warms the lumbar region before the backbend.

Marjariasana (Cat-Cow Stretch):
 Mobilises the entire spinal column and activates the erector spinae for the lift.

Setu Bandhasana Preparation (Partial Bridge):
 Lifts the hips only halfway, conditioning the glutes and hamstrings before the full Shoulder Pose.

Variations of Kandharasana

Variation 1: Ardha Kandharasana (Half Shoulder Pose)

Difficulty: Beginner

In this variation, lift the hips only halfway — just enough to slide a yoga block or folded blanket underneath the sacrum for support. The block holds the position passively, allowing the hip flexors and chest to open without demanding active muscular effort. This is an excellent entry point for complete beginners, those recovering from lower back sensitivity, or anyone on their first week of practice.

Variation 2: Eka Pada Kandharasana (Single-Leg Shoulder Pose)

Difficulty: Intermediate

From the full lifted position, extend one leg straight toward the ceiling while keeping the other foot grounded. This dramatically increases the demand on the standing-leg glute, hamstring, and stabilising muscles of the lower back. It also introduces an element of balance and hip-level control. Switch legs midway through the hold or alternate between sets.

Variation 3: Dynamic Kandharasana (Rhythmic Lift and Lower)

Difficulty: Beginner to Intermediate

Rather than holding the lifted position, continuously lift and lower the hips in sync with the breath — inhale to rise, exhale to lower — for 10 to 15 repetitions. This dynamic version functions more like a therapeutic spinal mobilisation exercise than a static stretch, and is particularly effective for those dealing with morning stiffness or sluggish digestion.

Variation 4: Kandharasana with Interlaced Fingers (Advanced Hold)

Difficulty: Intermediate to Advanced

In the full lifted position, interlace the fingers underneath the lower back and roll the outer edges of the shoulders further in, drawing the chest higher. This variation deepens the thoracic extension significantly and maximises the chest-opening effect. It requires considerable shoulder mobility and should only be attempted once the basic pose feels stable and comfortable.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Kandharasana

Letting the Knees Splay Outward
When the inner thighs are weak or inattentive, the knees drift apart during the lift, reducing glute activation and placing uneven load on the lower back. Keep the knees directly above the middle toes throughout the hold. Placing a yoga block between the thighs and gently squeezing it is a reliable cue.

Lifting Too High and Compressing the Lumbar
More height does not equal more benefit. When the hips are pushed beyond what the spine can comfortably support, the lower back shortens and compresses rather than lengthens. If you feel pinching or sharp sensation in the lumbar region, lower your hips by a centimetre or two until the sensation eases.

Holding the Breath
Many practitioners unconsciously hold their breath during the peak of the pose, which increases internal pressure and negates the calming effect on the nervous system. Practise breathing slowly and visibly — let the chest rise and fall even while the hips are elevated.

Rushing the Descent
Dropping the hips quickly from the lifted position removes the eccentric spinal load that makes the exercise therapeutic. Lower deliberately, one vertebra at a time, and feel the spine make contact with the mat sequentially from the upper back down to the sacrum.

Feet Too Far from the Hips
Placing the feet too far away reduces the mechanical advantage of the glutes and hamstrings, meaning the lower back compensates. Bring the heels close enough to your sitting bones that you could almost brush them with your fingertips when in the starting position.

Ignoring Shoulder Alignment
If the shoulders shrug upward toward the ears, the neck takes on excess strain and the chest cannot fully open. Actively draw the shoulder blades down the back before and throughout the lift. This one adjustment alone transforms the quality of the pose for most beginners.

Who Should Practise Kandharasana?

Those with Back Pain, Poor Posture, or Sedentary Lifestyles
Kandharasana is one of the most recommended poses for people who experience chronic lower back tension from prolonged sitting. The pose strengthens the posterior chain — glutes, erector spinae, and hamstrings — which together support the lumbar spine. With consistent daily practice, many people notice a gradual reduction in the stiffness and fatigue associated with desk-bound routines. This is best pursued as a complement to medical guidance, not a replacement for it.

Those Supporting Digestive Health or Hormonal Balance
The abdominal stimulation and increased pelvic blood flow created by the Shoulder Pose make it particularly relevant for people supporting digestive regularity or managing hormonal imbalances. Women experiencing menstrual irregularities, pelvic tension, or reproductive health goals often find this pose a valuable addition to their routine when practised consistently over time.

Is Kandharasana Good for Beginners?
Yes — Kandharasana is one of the most beginner-accessible backbends in yoga. Unlike poses that demand shoulder or wrist flexibility, the Shoulder Pose requires only that you can lie on your back comfortably and lift your hips. The Ardha (half) variation makes it even more approachable. Beginners benefit most from practising it slowly, with close attention to breath and alignment, rather than focusing on the height of the lift.

Intermediate Practitioners Building a Consistent Morning Routine
For those who already have some yoga experience, Kandharasana serves as an anchor pose in a morning sequence — one that wakes up the spine, opens the chest, and shifts the nervous system into a calm, ready state before the day begins. Combined with standing poses and pranayama, it forms the foundation of a well-rounded daily practice.

Make Kandharasana a Part of Your Life

Kandharasana — the Shoulder Pose — is a supine backbend that strengthens the lower back, opens the chest, stimulates the digestive and reproductive organs, and calms the nervous system. Its benefits are wide-ranging, making it suited to beginners, desk workers, women managing hormonal health, and anyone building a consistent daily yoga practice.

Whether you are a complete beginner or returning to yoga after a gap, Kandharasana is accessible with the right guidance. The half variation removes any pressure to perform a deep backbend immediately, and live corrections from an experienced instructor ensure you build the pose safely from day one.
