What is Agnisar Kriya?

Agnisar kriya — agni meaning “fire” and sar meaning “essence” — is one of the six classical shatkarma purification practices in Hatha yoga, described in the Gheranda Samhita (1.19) as one of the most powerful practices for stimulating digestive fire. It is closely related to uddiyana bandha (the abdominal lock) but adds a rapid contraction-and-release pumping action that no static lock can match. Research published in the Journal of Ayurveda and Integrative Medicine documents measurable improvements in digestive enzyme activity and gastric motility among practitioners of abdominal shatkarma techniques over sustained practice periods.

The practice sits within the broader family of yogic cleansing techniques. Where

The mechanic is simple: exhale fully, hold the breath out, then rapidly snap the abdomen in and out 30 to

 before inhaling. That repeated pumping is what gives the practice its name — it kindles the inner fire.

How to Do Agnisar Kriya — Step-by-Step

Done correctly, agnisar kriya takes 
5 minutes
. The mechanic is simple but the precision matters — specifically the breath sequencing.

Step 1: Empty Stomach Required

Practise on an empty stomach — early morning is ideal. At least 
4 hours
 after a meal otherwise.

Step 2: Standing Forward Position

Stand with feet shoulder-width apart. Bend forward slightly. Place hands on the thighs just above the knees, fingers pointing inward, elbows locked.

Step 3: Full Inhale, Then Full Exhale

Inhale slowly and deeply. Exhale completely through the mouth — push every last bit of air out.

Step 4: Hold the Breath Out

Without inhaling, hold the breath out. The abdominal wall will naturally hollow inward. This is the critical step — the hold must be after a full exhale, never after an inhale.

Step 5: Pump the Abdomen

Rapidly contract and release the abdominal wall — pull the navel toward the spine, then release fully. Repeat 
20–30 times
 in this single breath hold.

Step 6: Inhale Slowly and Rest

When you need air, release the lock and inhale slowly through the nose. Rest for 
30 seconds
 with normal breathing. Never gasp — the pace should feel comfortable.

Step 7: Repeat for 3 Rounds

That is one round. Beginners do 
3 rounds
 of 20 pumps. Build over 4–6 weeks to 
5 rounds
 of 50 pumps.

Preparatory Steps Before Agnisar Kriya

Practise basic uddiyana bandha for 1–2 weeks first — without abdominal lock control, the pumping won’t engage the correct muscle layer.

Warm up with 10 cat-cow rounds — mobilises the spine and warms the abdominal wall before pumping.

Confirm hydration and empty bowels — both improve the mechanical effect of the practice.

Variations of Agnisar Kriya

Beginner Standing Variation

The standing forward position described above — easiest entry point and the form most beginners should hold for 4–6 weeks.

Seated Vajrasana Variation (Intermediate)

Performed in Vajrasana with hands on thighs, slightly leaning forward. More demanding on breath retention but gentler on the lower back for those with lumbar issues.

Nauli Kriya (Advanced)

The most advanced extension — the abdominal pumping turns into a rolling motion of the rectus abdominis. Requires 3–6 months of consistent agnisar mastery before attempting.

Combined with Kapalbhati

A common sequence: 60 strokes of kapalbhati, rest 
30 seconds
, then 
3 rounds
 of agnisar. Used for accelerated digestive and weight benefits when the body is ready for the combined load.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Agnisar Kriya

Mistake 1: Pumping with breath held in instead of out. This is the most common beginner error. The hold must be after a full exhale, never after an inhale.

Mistake 2: Shallow abdominal contractions. Half-effort gives half-results. The navel should snap inward toward the spine on each pump.

Mistake 3: Locked knees and rigid spine. A slight forward bend with relaxed knees lets the abdomen move freely.

Mistake 4: Practising on a full stomach. Defeats the cleansing effect and risks nausea and discomfort.

Mistake 5: Forcing too many pumps in one round. Build gradually — quality of contraction beats quantity every time.

Mistake 6: Holding the breath too long. The hold should feel comfortable, not desperate. Inhale before strain sets in.

How is Agnisar Kriya Different from Kapalbhati?

Kapalbhati is breath-driven — rapid forceful exhalations move the upper abdomen. Agnisar kriya is performed during a full breath retention after exhalation and pumps the deeper abdomen mechanically. Both are shatkarmas; they target different layers and complement each other powerfully in sequence.

Can Pregnant Women or Women on Their Period Do Agnisar Kriya?

The practice is best paused during menstruation (the abdominal pumping is intense) and avoided throughout pregnancy. Resume gently 6 weeks postpartum with appropriate guidance from a certified instructor.
