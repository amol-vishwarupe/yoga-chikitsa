Eka Pada Koundinyasana — the One-Legged Arm Balance of Sage Koundinya — is an advanced arm balance that combines twisting, arm strength, and hip flexibility in a single suspended pose. Named after the sage Kaundinya (or Koundinya), one of the Buddha’s first disciples, the pose exists in two expressions: Eka Pada Koundinyasana I (from a twisted lunge) and Eka Pada Koundinyasana II (from a straight-on position). Both require exceptional arm endurance, core integration, and open hips.

What is Eka Pada Koundinyasana?

Eka Pada Koundinyasana — pronounced eh-KAH pah-DAH kown-din-YAHS-ana — translates as One-Legged Pose Dedicated to Sage Koundinya (Eka = one, Pada = leg, Koundinya = the sage’s name, Asana = posture). The pose belongs to the twisted arm balance family alongside Parsva Bakasana (Side Crow Pose) — both requiring the oblique body rotation that distinguishes twisted arm balances from their forward-facing counterparts.

In Eka Pada Koundinyasana I, one leg hooks over the upper arm while the opposite leg extends back in a Chaturanga-like position — the twist of the spine creating the hook. In Eka Pada Koundinyasana II, one leg extends forward while the other extends back, creating a flying split on the arms without the twisting component. At Habuild, both versions are taught as part of the advanced arm balance progression following Bakasana and Chaturanga Dandasana mastery.

Eka Pada Koundinyasana Benefits

Physical Benefits

Develops Twisted Arm Balance Strength
Eka Pada Koundinyasana requires the arm and shoulder strength of

Chaturanga Dandasana sustained in a rotated, asymmetric position — the twisted load creating greater demands on the stabilising shoulder musculature than symmetrical arm balances. This builds the rotational stability that advanced practice requires.
Opens the Hip and Develops Spinal Rotation

The hooking of one leg over the upper arm requires significant hip flexor and external rotator flexibility — complemented by the thoracic rotation of the twist. This combined hip-and-spine opening is a meaningful complement to
yoga for flexibility work and the individual hip and spine practices that prepare Koundinyasana components separately.

Builds Core Strength and Rotational Stability
Holding the body suspended in a twisted position requires sustained oblique and deep core engagement — the rotational core strength that most yoga practices develop less directly than frontal core work. This complements

Navasana and other frontal core practices for comprehensive core conditioning.
Improves Wrist and Forearm Endurance

The full body weight supported through the wrists in an asymmetric position creates significant wrist and forearm endurance demands — building the joint health and muscular support that protect the wrists in sustained arm balance practice.

Mental and Emotional Benefits

Develops Commitment and Trust in the Entry
Eka Pada Koundinyasana requires a specific committed lean forward into the arms — a point of no-return entry that demands the same fearless trust that all advanced arm balances cultivate. Consistent practice builds this quality of committed, non-hesitant action.

How to Do Eka Pada Koundinyasana I — Step-by-Step

Key Principles

Enter from a High Lunge with twist — never attempt directly from standing. The forward knee shoulder-hooks before the back leg extends. The weight must shift significantly forward over the hands before the back foot leaves the ground. The arm shelf must be created deliberately — compress the front thigh firmly against the upper arm before the lean.

Step 1: Begin in High Lunge with Twist
From Warrior I with the right foot forward: exhale and twist the torso to the right. Bring the left elbow outside the right knee — the classic Parivrtta Parsvakonasana preparation.

Step 2: Plant the Hands and Create the Shoulder Shelf
Place both palms firmly on the floor, shoulder-width apart. Press the right thigh firmly against the back of the right upper arm — this contact is the shelf that will support the leg.

Step 3: Lean Forward and Bend the Elbows
Lean the body weight significantly forward over the hands. Bend the elbows toward Chaturanga position — the elbows tracking directly back over the wrists. The right foot will begin to lighten as the weight transfers.

Step 4: Lift the Back Leg and Extend
On an exhale: lift the left (back) foot and extend the left leg straight back — parallel to the floor or slightly above it. The right leg remains hooked over the right upper arm.

Step 5: Hold and Release with Control
Hold for 3–5 breaths — gaze forward, breathing steady. To release: bend the elbows slightly and lower the feet to the floor with control. Repeat on the left side.

Breathing

The entry exhale initiates the lean and the back leg lift. Once suspended, breathe steadily — the breath is somewhat compressed by the twisted position. Never hold the breath; the steady breath maintains the parasympathetic composure within the demanding pose.

Preparatory Poses

Bakasana (Crow Pose) — establish comfortable arm balance foundation

Parsva Bakasana (Side Crow) — introduces the twist + arm balance combination

Parivrtta Parsvakonasana — develops the thoracic twist and hip opening of the entry

Ardha Matsyendrasana — spinal rotation and hip opening

Chaturanga Dandasana — build arm strength endurance for the sustained hold

Variations of Eka Pada Koundinyasana

Variation 1: Eka Pada Koundinyasana I — Twisted Version
From a twisted lunge entry — one leg hooks over the upper arm while the other extends back. The spinal rotation creates the hook. This is typically learned first as the entry mechanics are more intuitive.

Variation 2: Eka Pada Koundinyasana II — Split Arm Balance
Without the twist — both legs split in opposite directions on the arms. One leg hooks forward, one extends back — creating a flying split balance without the rotational component. Requires both extreme hip flexibility and arm strength.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Insufficient forward lean — the most common failure; the weight must shift very far forward before the back foot can lift

Insufficient thigh-to-arm contact — the leg must press firmly against the arm shelf before attempting the lift

Collapsing the chest — maintain active chest pressing and serratus engagement throughout

Attempting without established Bakasana — Crow Pose comfort is the minimum prerequisite

Only practising one side — always balance with the left-side entry

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Eka Pada Koundinyasana?

Eka Pada Koundinyasana (One-Legged Sage Koundinya’s Pose) is an advanced twisted arm balance in which one leg hooks over the upper arm while the other extends back — the body suspended on both hands. It exists in two versions: a twisted version (Koundinyasana I) and a straight split version (Koundinyasana II).

How do you do Eka Pada Koundinyasana?

Enter from a High Lunge with a deep thoracic twist. Plant both palms and press the front thigh firmly against the upper arm. Lean very far forward over the hands, bend the elbows, and lift the back foot — extending the back leg. Hold 3–5 breaths, then release with control and repeat on the other side.

What are the benefits of Eka Pada Koundinyasana?

Benefits include twisted arm balance strength, hip and spinal rotation flexibility, oblique and deep core strengthening, wrist and forearm endurance, and the committed, fearless entry that advanced arm balance practice develops. These benefits accumulate through the preparatory progression as much as through the full pose.

What is the difference between Eka Pada Koundinyasana I and II?

Eka Pada Koundinyasana I involves a spinal twist — the body rotates and the front leg hooks over the upper arm. Koundinyasana II does not involve a twist — both legs split in opposite directions on the arms in a straight flying split position. Koundinyasana I is typically learned first; Koundinyasana II requires greater hip flexibility for the split position.

What is eka pada yoga?

‘Eka pada’ means ‘one leg’ in Sanskrit and refers to any yoga posture where one leg is the primary action — either extended, raised, or specifically positioned in a distinct way from the other. Eka Pada Koundinyasana, Eka Pada Raja Kapotasana (one-legged pigeon), and Eka Pada Sirsasana (leg behind head) are all ‘eka pada’ (one-leg) yoga poses.
