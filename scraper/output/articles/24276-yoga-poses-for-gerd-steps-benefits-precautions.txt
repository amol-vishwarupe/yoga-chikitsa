Yoga Poses for GERD (Acid Reflux): Steps, Benefits & Precautions

Yoga poses for GERD are a curated set of restorative asanas, gentle twists, and forward folds that work on the abdominal and thoracic region to support digestive health, reduce abdominal pressure, and calm the stress responses that trigger excess acid production. Practised consistently on an empty stomach, these poses complement medical care for Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease.

What are Yoga Poses for GERD?

GERD — Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease — is a condition where stomach acid repeatedly flows back into the oesophagus, causing discomfort, a burning sensation, and bloating. Yoga poses for GERD are a curated set of asanas traditionally used to support digestive health, reduce abdominal tension, and calm the nervous system. Pronounced as “jerd” in common usage, the condition affects millions of adults in India and worldwide.

Within the broader yoga system, these poses belong primarily to the family of forward folds, twists, and restorative postures. Each works on the abdominal and thoracic region in ways that may support digestive function — compressing, stretching, or gently massaging the organs involved in digestion. Ancient yogic texts have long recognised the relationship between posture, breath, and the digestive fire (Agni).

Practising these asanas regularly as part of a guided morning routine — rather than as isolated stretches — is what allows them to gradually support how your digestive system functions day to day. The goal is not a single session but a consistent habit.

Yoga Poses for GERD — Benefits

Physical Benefits

Benefit 1: Reduces Abdominal Pressure and Bloating
Several GERD yoga exercises — particularly gentle forward folds and knee-to-chest poses — gently compress the abdomen, helping to release trapped gas and ease the bloated feeling that often accompanies acid reflux. With regular practice, the abdominal muscles become better toned, reducing the chronic intra-abdominal pressure that pushes acid upward. This is one of the most directly felt physical benefits for GERD practitioners.

Benefit 2: Strengthens the Diaphragm and Core
Poses like 
Setu Bandhasana
 and Viparita Karani gently engage the diaphragm — the muscle that, when weak, can contribute to a hiatal hernia and acid backflow. Strengthening the diaphragm through breath-coordinated yoga movements supports the lower oesophageal sphincter’s ability to stay closed. A stronger core also reduces the postural collapse that compresses the stomach.

Benefit 3: Improves Overall Digestive Circulation
Twisting poses such as 
Supta Matsyendrasana
 gently stimulate the digestive organs by improving blood circulation to the stomach, liver, and intestines. Better circulation supports more efficient digestion, which in turn may reduce the likelihood of acid buildup. Acid reflux yoga poses that include twists are particularly valued for this organ-massage effect.

Benefit 4: Calms the Vagus Nerve and Reduces Stress-Triggered Reflux
Stress is one of the most underappreciated triggers of GERD flare-ups. Restorative poses like Viparita Karani (Legs Up the Wall) and slow diaphragmatic breathing activate the parasympathetic nervous system via the vagus nerve, shifting the body out of “fight or flight” mode. When the nervous system is calmer, the digestive system is better able to function without triggering excess acid production.

Benefit 5: Builds Awareness of Posture and Eating Habits
A consistent yoga practice naturally develops body awareness — you start noticing when you are slouching, eating too fast, or breathing shallowly. These are all habits that worsen GERD over time. Through daily practice, many Habuild members report becoming more mindful around meal times and posture, which complements the physical benefits of the poses themselves.

Mental and Emotional Benefits

How to Do Yoga Poses for GERD — Step-by-Step Instructions

The sequence below walks you through the most recommended GERD yoga exercises. Practice these on an empty stomach — ideally 
2–3 hours
 after a meal — and never force any position that causes discomfort in the chest or throat.

Key Principles

Always breathe through the nose. Move slowly and with control. Avoid inversions that place the head below the heart if your reflux is acute. If any pose worsens your symptoms, come out immediately and rest in a neutral position. Consistency over intensity is the governing principle here.

Step 1: Starting Position — Supta Baddha Konasana (Reclined Butterfly)

Lie flat on your back with the soles of your feet together and knees falling out to the sides. Place one hand on your chest and one on your belly. Take 5 slow breaths, feeling the belly rise and fall. This opening pose activates the parasympathetic system and prepares your digestive organs for movement. Feel a gentle release in the lower abdomen with each exhale.

Step 2: Pawanmuktasana (Wind-Relieving Pose)

From your back, draw both knees into the chest and wrap your arms around the shins. On an exhale, gently press the knees closer to the abdomen. Hold for 5–8 breaths. This is one of the most targeted acid reflux yoga poses — the abdominal compression helps release trapped gas and can ease the bloating associated with reflux. Keep the lower back pressing gently into the floor.

Step 3: Supta Matsyendrasana (Supine Spinal Twist)

Still lying on your back, extend your arms in a T-shape. Bend the right knee and let it fall across the body to the left, turning your gaze to the right. Hold for 5 breaths, then switch sides. This gentle twist massages the abdominal organs and encourages peristalsis — the movement of food through the digestive tract. Alignment cue: keep both shoulders on the floor throughout.

Step 4: Setu Bandhasana (Bridge Pose)

Roll up to your back with knees bent and feet hip-width apart, flat on the floor. On an inhale, press through the feet and lift the hips toward the ceiling, engaging the glutes and inner thighs. Hold for 4–5 breaths before lowering slowly. Bridge pose strengthens the diaphragm and lower back while opening the chest — both important for long-term GERD management. Avoid this if you have acute reflux symptoms on the day of practice.

Step 5: Final Position — Viparita Karani (Legs Up the Wall)

Move to a wall and swing your legs up so they rest vertically against it, with your back flat on the floor and arms relaxed at your sides. Stay here for 
5–10 minutes
 with slow, natural breathing. This restorative pose is a cornerstone among yoga poses for GERD — it calms the nervous system, reduces stress-related acid production, and improves venous return without inverting the stomach. You should feel a deep sense of ease throughout the abdominal region.

Step 6: How to Come Out of the Sequence

From Viparita Karani, bend the knees and roll slowly to one side. Pause in a foetal position for a few breaths before pressing up to seated. Never spring up quickly from the floor after this sequence — give your system a moment to re-orient. Transition into Savasana (Corpse Pose) for 
3–5 minutes
 to allow full integration before standing.

Breathing in Yoga Poses for GERD

Diaphragmatic breathing — where the belly expands fully on the inhale and gently contracts on the exhale — is the foundation of every pose in this sequence. Chest-only breathing bypasses the diaphragm and does not provide the toning and calming effect needed to support GERD management. Practice a 4-count inhale and 6-count exhale as a general guide throughout. Never hold the breath forcefully in any of these poses.

Preparatory Poses Before Your GERD Yoga Practice

Warming up the relevant muscle groups — particularly the hips, lower back, and abdominal region — helps you move into the main sequence safely and with greater ease.

Balasana (Child’s Pose)
 — gently stretches the lower back and relaxes the abdomen before deeper work.

Marjaryasana-Bitilasana (Cat-Cow)
 — mobilises the spine and massages the digestive organs rhythmically with the breath.

Tadasana (Mountain Pose)
 — establishes upright posture and diaphragmatic breathing as a baseline before floor work.

Seated Side Stretch
 — opens the intercostal muscles and the sides of the torso, creating space around the stomach.

Variations of Yoga Poses for GERD

Variation 1: Ardha Pawanmuktasana (Half Wind-Relieving Pose) — Beginner Level

Instead of drawing both knees to the chest, work with one knee at a time. This is easier on the lower back and gentler for those new to yoga or with significant bloating. It still delivers the abdominal compression benefit but with less intensity. Alternate sides for 5 breaths each.

Variation 2: Supported Viparita Karani (Bolster Under Hips) — Beginner to Intermediate

Place a folded blanket or bolster beneath the hips before swinging the legs up the wall. This slight elevation deepens the passive stretch in the lower back and further reduces tension in the digestive region. Ideal for those who find the flat-floor version uncomfortable.

Variation 3: Seated Supta Matsyendrasana (Chair Twist) — Office-Friendly Adaptation

For those who spend long hours sitting and cannot get onto the floor mid-day, the supine spinal twist can be adapted as a seated chair twist. Sit upright, cross the right leg over the left knee, and gently rotate the torso to the right, placing the left hand on the right knee. This GERD-supportive twist is effective and accessible anywhere. Difficulty: Beginner.

Variation 4: Elevated Upper-Body Savasana — Rest Adaptation

For those who experience reflux even lying flat, rest in Savasana with a bolster or folded blankets under the upper back and head so the torso is at a 30-degree incline. This allows the full relaxation benefit without putting the oesophagus in a position where gravity works against it. Difficulty: Beginner — particularly suitable during active flare-ups.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Yoga Poses for GERD

1. Practising immediately after a meal

This is the most common mistake. Always wait at least 
2–3 hours
 after eating before practising any GERD yoga exercises. Moving the body with a full stomach increases intra-abdominal pressure and can worsen reflux.

2. Performing strong inversions during active flare-ups

Headstands, shoulder stands, and deep backbends where the stomach drops below the oesophagus should be avoided when symptoms are active. Stick to the restorative sequence above until the flare calms.

3. Holding the breath or forcing exhalations

Forceful pranayama techniques such as Kapalabhati increase intra-abdominal pressure sharply and are not appropriate for GERD management. Keep breathing soft, slow, and diaphragmatic throughout.

4. Rushing through poses

Spending only 2–3 breaths in each pose does not allow the nervous system to shift into a parasympathetic state — the very shift that supports digestive function. Hold each pose for a minimum of 5 breaths to receive the benefit.

5. Wearing tight clothing around the waist

Tight waistbands during practice increase pressure on the lower oesophageal sphincter. Wear loose, comfortable clothing — especially around the abdomen — for all acid reflux yoga poses.

6. Skipping Savasana

The integration phase at the end of practice is not optional. The nervous system needs 
3–5 minutes
 of stillness to complete the parasympathetic shift that makes the session therapeutic for GERD. Do not leave the mat early.

Who Should Practise Yoga Poses for GERD?

Those with Acid Reflux, Bloating, or Digestive Discomfort
People who experience regular heartburn, post-meal heaviness, or bloating are the primary audience for this practice. These yoga poses for GERD work by gently reducing abdominal pressure, supporting digestion, and calming the stress responses that trigger excess acid. They complement — not replace — any medical treatment already in place. Always consult your doctor before starting if you have a confirmed GERD diagnosis.

Those with Back Pain or Postural Problems
Poor posture — particularly rounded upper back and forward head position — compresses the stomach and worsens reflux. People dealing with back pain or desk-related postural issues often find that working on spinal alignment through yoga simultaneously supports better management of GERD symptoms. Explore 
yoga practices specifically designed to support back pain management
 as a complementary programme. The spine-lengthening and diaphragm-strengthening effects carry over directly to GERD relief.

Is This Practice Good for Beginners?
Absolutely. Every pose in this sequence has a beginner-friendly variation, and none of them require prior yoga experience. The most important thing is breath awareness and moving slowly — both of which beginners can develop from their very first session. If you are new to yoga altogether, starting with 
a beginner-friendly yoga practice
 will build the body awareness that makes the GERD sequence more effective.

Working Professionals and High-Stress Individuals
Stress-related GERD is extremely common among people in demanding jobs. A 20–30 minute morning yoga session — done consistently every day — addresses both the physical and the emotional triggers of acid reflux. Frequently Asked Questions About Yoga Poses for GERD

What is GERD yoga?

GERD yoga refers to a curated selection of asanas — primarily restorative poses, gentle twists, and forward folds — that support digestive health and may help manage the discomfort associated with Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease. The practice works by reducing abdominal pressure, calming the nervous system, and supporting better diaphragm tone over time.

Is yoga for GERD good for beginners?

Yes. The poses in a GERD yoga sequence are among the gentlest in the entire yoga repertoire. Every asana has a beginner variation, and none require flexibility or prior experience. The focus is on breath awareness and slow, mindful movement — both accessible from day one.

What is the difference between yoga for GERD and Hatha yoga?

Hatha yoga is a broad, classical system encompassing hundreds of asanas and breathing practices. Yoga for GERD is a targeted sub-selection of Hatha poses chosen specifically for their effect on the digestive system and nervous system. A general Hatha class may include poses that are unsuitable for GERD (such as strong backbends or inversions during flare-ups), whereas a GERD-focused sequence avoids these.

Can yoga help with managing GERD symptoms over time?

A consistent daily yoga practice may gradually support better management of GERD symptoms by reducing stress (a key trigger), improving diaphragm tone, encouraging healthy digestion, and building body awareness around posture and eating habits. It complements medical care — it is not a substitute for it. Always continue any prescribed treatment while practising.

How many calories does yoga for GERD burn?

A 30-minute restorative yoga session burns approximately 80–150 calories depending on body weight and the intensity of the sequence. The primary purpose of GERD yoga is not calorie expenditure but nervous system regulation and digestive support. Weight management benefits, if any, come from the cumulative effect of daily consistent practice.

How often should I practise yoga poses for GERD?

Daily practice delivers the most consistent benefit. Even 
20–30 minutes
 each morning, on an empty stomach, is enough to build the diaphragmatic strength and nervous system habits that support GERD management over time. Sporadic practice is far less effective than a daily routine.

What should I wear for a GERD yoga class?

Wear loose, comfortable clothing — specifically avoiding tight waistbands, belts, or anything that compresses the abdominal area. Tight clothing around the waist increases pressure on the lower oesophageal sphincter and can worsen symptoms. Breathable, stretch fabrics that allow full diaphragmatic breathing are ideal.
