What is Neti Kriya?

Neti kriya is one of the six classical shatkarma cleansing practices of hatha yoga. The Sanskrit word neti refers to nasal cleansing, and kriya means “action” or “process”. The practice involves passing warm saline water (jal neti) or a soft thread (sutra neti) through the nasal passages to clear accumulated mucus, dust, and pollutants. Of the two main forms, jal neti is by far the most accessible and is widely practised today as a daily wellness ritual. Neti kriya yoga is described in classical texts including the Hatha Yoga Pradipika, where it is recommended as a preparatory practice for pranayama and meditation.

In modern terms, neti kriya provides a mechanical flushing of the nasal passages that is similar to the saline rinse devices recommended by ENT specialists for sinus health. The practice has gained mainstream acceptance, with neti pots available in pharmacies and supermarkets across most countries. Beyond its sinus-clearing function, daily neti practice is traditionally believed to improve breath quality, support meditation, and reduce headache frequency. Neti Kriya Benefits

Physical Benefits

Benefit 1: Clears Nasal Passages and Supports Sinus Health
The mechanical flushing action removes accumulated mucus, dust, and allergens from the nasal passages. Many practitioners report reduced seasonal allergy symptoms and clearer breathing.

Benefit 2: Supports Recovery from Mild Cold and Sinus Symptoms
Daily neti during early stages of a cold can ease congestion. The benefits of neti kriya for sinus health are widely documented in both traditional and modern sources.

Benefit 3: Improves Breath Quality for Pranayama
Clear nasal passages allow the slow, deep breathing that pranayama requires. Practitioners often report easier breath work after starting daily neti.

Benefit 4: Supports Meditation and Concentration
Cleared breathing pathways support the steady breath that meditation requires. The traditional view connects clear breath to clear mind.

Benefit 5: Builds a Daily Wellness Ritual
Morning neti becomes a structured wellness habit that often supports broader healthy routines.

Mental and Emotional Benefits

How to Practise Neti Kriya. Step-by-Step Instructions

Step 1: Prepare the Saline Water

Mix half a teaspoon of clean (preferably non-iodised) salt into 250 ml of warm distilled or previously boiled water. The water should be lukewarm, similar to body temperature.

Step 2: Starting Position

Stand over a sink. Tilt your head sideways so one ear points downward toward the sink. Keep the mouth open and breathe through it throughout the practice.

Step 3: Insert the Neti Pot Spout

Place the spout gently into the upper nostril. Keep the lips firmly together and breathe only through the open mouth.

Step 4: Pour the Water Slowly

Gently tilt the neti pot so the water flows into the upper nostril. The water will travel through the nasal passages and exit through the lower nostril. Half the water (about 125 ml) per side is the typical amount.

Step 5: Final Position and Drainage

Once half the water has flowed through, gently exhale through the nose to clear the passages. Switch sides and repeat with the remaining water.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Neti Kriya

After completing both sides, blow the nose gently to clear residual moisture. Some practitioners follow with a few rounds of kapalbhati to dry the nasal passages completely.

Breathing in Neti Kriya

Mouth breathing throughout the water flow. Nasal breathing only after the nose has been cleared and dried. Trying to breathe through the nose during the water flow causes coughing and is unsafe.

Preparatory Practices Before Neti Kriya (Optional)

Brief preparation makes the practice more comfortable.

Drink a glass of water: Hydrate the body before any cleansing practice.

5 cycles of slow breathing: Settle the nervous system for the focused practice.

Gentle neck stretches: Loosen the neck for the side-tilt position.

Variations of Neti Kriya

Variation 1: Jal Neti (Water Neti. Beginner-Safe)

The water-based version described in the step-by-step section above. The most accessible and widely practised type of neti kriya. Detailed in our work on 
jal neti benefits
.

Variation 2: Sutra Neti (Thread Neti. Advanced)

A soft cotton or rubber thread passed through one nostril and pulled out through the mouth. Removes deeper mucus that water cannot reach. Advanced practice that requires direct teacher supervision and should not be attempted from videos.

Variation 3: Dugdha Neti (Milk Neti. Specialised)

Warm milk used in place of saline water. A specialised variant used for specific therapeutic purposes under teacher guidance.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Neti Kriya

Mistake 1: Using Untreated Tap Water
Tap water has been linked to rare but serious nasal infections. Always use distilled, sterile, or previously boiled water that has cooled to lukewarm.

Mistake 2: Using Water That is Too Hot or Too Cold
Both extremes are uncomfortable and can shock the nasal passages. Lukewarm body temperature is the right temperature.

Mistake 3: Skipping the Drying Step
Water remaining in the nasal passages can drip later or contribute to a temporary blockage. Always clear the passages thoroughly with gentle blowing.

Mistake 4: Practising with an Active Sinus Infection
Neti kriya during an active sinus infection can spread infection. Wait until acute symptoms have resolved or follow medical guidance.

Who Should Practise Neti Kriya?

Adults with Seasonal Allergies
Daily neti during allergy seasons reduces symptoms for many practitioners. The mechanical flushing removes the pollen and dust that drive allergic responses.

Adults with Chronic Sinus Sluggishness
Long-standing nasal congestion often improves with daily neti. Always rule out infection or structural issues with a doctor first.

Yoga Practitioners
The clearer breath supports pranayama and meditation. Many serious yoga students practise jal neti daily.

Is Neti Kriya Good for Beginners?
Yes. Jal neti is accessible from day one with a neti pot and proper instruction. The first few attempts may feel strange but most practitioners adapt within a week.

Frequently Asked Questions about Neti Kriya

What is Neti Kriya?

Neti kriya is one of the six classical shatkarmas (cleansing practices) of hatha yoga. The most common form is jal neti, which uses warm saline water to cleanse the nasal passages.

Is Neti Kriya Good for Beginners?

Yes. Jal neti is accessible from day one with a neti pot and proper instruction.

What Are the Benefits of Neti Kriya?

Benefits of neti kriya include clearer nasal passages, reduced allergy symptoms, improved breathing for pranayama, and support during early-stage colds.

What is Neti Kriya Yoga?

Neti kriya yoga refers to the integration of neti practice within the broader hatha yoga tradition. It is a preparatory cleansing practice that supports asana, pranayama, and meditation.

What Are the Types of Neti Kriya?

The three types are jal neti (water-based, beginner-safe), sutra neti (thread-based, advanced), and dugdha neti (milk-based, specialised).

How Often Should I Practise Neti Kriya?

Daily morning practice is traditional. Some practitioners continue throughout the day during heavy allergy seasons.

Can I Practise Neti Kriya at Home?

Yes, with proper instruction. Use only distilled or previously boiled water and a clean neti pot.

Is Neti Kriya Safe?

Jal neti with proper water and technique is safe for most adults. People with sinus infections, recent nasal surgery, or chronic ear problems should consult a doctor first.
