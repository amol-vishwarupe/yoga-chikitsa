Utthita Parsvakonasana, or Extended Side Angle Pose, creates a long lateral diagonal line stretching the intercostals, obliques, and groin while building lower-body strength, toning the waist, and opening the hips. It supports spinal lateral mobility and supports weight management. Suitable for all fitness levels with modifications.

What is Utthita Parsvakonasana?

Utthita Parsvakonasana — known in English as Extended Side Angle Pose — derives from Sanskrit: Utthita (extended or stretched), Parsva (side or flank), Kona (angle), and Asana (posture). From a wide-legged stance with the front knee bent, the lower hand reaches toward the floor and the upper arm extends overhead — creating a continuous diagonal line from the back foot to the extended fingertips.

Utthita Parsvakonasana is one of yoga’s most comprehensive standing postures — simultaneously a standing strength exercise, a deep lateral body stretch, a hip opener, and a cardiovascular challenge.

Benefits

Physical Benefits

Develops Full-Body Strength and Endurance
The loaded lateral stance simultaneously works the quadriceps, glutes, hip abductors, lateral core, and shoulder stabilisers — one of the most metabolically effective standing postures for functional strength and body composition.

Stretches the Lateral Body, Groin, and Intercostal Muscles
The extended lateral line provides one of the deepest lateral body stretches in yoga — lengthening the obliques, intercostals, quadratus lumborum, and lateral hip muscles in a sustained, loaded position.

Tones the Waist and Supports Abdominal Health
The sustained lateral oblique engagement — inner obliques compressing on the bent-knee side, outer obliques stretching on the extended side — directly tones the waist over consistent practice.

Opens the Hips and Improves Groin Flexibility
The wide-legged bent-knee stance opens the groin and inner thigh of the front leg while stretching the hip flexor of the back leg — bilateral hip work that progressive yoga postures depend upon.

Mental Benefits

Builds Focused Determination
Utthita Parsvakonasana held for 5–10 breaths is genuinely demanding — maintaining the full extension and the breath through this discomfort builds the focused determination that yoga systematically develops.

How to Do Utthita Parsvakonasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Key Principles Three alignment principles: the extended lateral line from back heel to fingertips is one unbroken diagonal; the front knee tracks over the second toe — never collapsing inward; and the chest faces forward or upward — never toward the floor.

Utthita Parsvakonasana — Step by Step Step 1: Starting in Virabhadrasana II
Begin in Virabhadrasana II — right foot forward, left foot turned in, arms extending laterally at shoulder height. This is the standard entry into Utthita Parsvakonasana.

Step 2: Lower the Right Forearm or Hand
On the exhale, bring the right forearm to rest on the right thigh (beginner), or the right hand to a block outside the right foot, or flat on the floor.

Step 3: Extend the Left Arm Overhead
Extend the left arm directly overhead — reaching past the left ear in a diagonal line from the back heel to the fingertips.

Step 4: Rotate the Chest Open
Turn the chest toward the ceiling — not toward the floor. The entire body from the left heel to the left fingertips forms one unbroken lateral diagonal.

Step 5: Breathe Into the Upper Ribcage
Hold for 5–8 breath cycles, breathing into the expanded upper (left) ribcage on each inhale. Each inhale creates more space in the lateral body.

Step 6: Rise and Repeat Other Side
Inhale to rise back to Virabhadrasana II. Reverse the foot position and repeat on the left side.

Breathing

Breathing into the upper (extended) intercostal space on the inhale creates the progressive lateral opening. Each inhale expands the upper ribcage laterally; each exhale settles the body more deeply into the stance without losing the extended line.

Preparatory Poses

Virabhadrasana II (5-8 breaths) — The direct entry — establishes the wide-legged bent-knee stance before the lateral extension.

Trikonasana (Triangle Pose) — The straight-leg companion — warms the hamstrings and lateral body before the bent-knee expression.

Prasarita Padottanasana — Warms the inner thighs and wide-legged awareness.

Variations

Variation 1: Forearm on Thigh — Most Accessible
Right forearm resting on right thigh — reduces the lateral tilt required and allows focus on front knee alignment and upper arm reach.

Variation 2: Hand on Block — Intermediate
A block at the appropriate height outside the front foot — allows the full lateral line without requiring the hamstring and hip flexibility for hand-to-floor contact.

Variation 3: Full Expression with Bind — Advanced
The lower arm wraps under the front thigh while the upper arm wraps behind the back — hands clasping. Adds a deep shoulder and chest opener to the lateral stance.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Chest Facing the Floor Rather Than the Ceiling
The most visually obvious error. The chest must rotate open toward the ceiling — not fall toward the floor — to develop the spinal rotation and lateral body opening the pose is designed to create.

Front Knee Collapsing Inward
The front knee must track actively over the second or third toe throughout the hold. Knee collapse concentrates medial knee strain and reduces the hip abductor engagement.

Body Weight in the Lower Hand
The lower hand is a balance guide only — not a weight-bearing support. Significant body weight in the lower hand bypasses the lateral core engagement.

Who Should Practise?

Those Seeking Lateral Body Flexibility and Strength
Utthita Parsvakonasana fills the lateral body opening gap that most yoga sequences leave — providing the intercostal, oblique, and lateral hip flexibility that balances forward-bending dominant practice.

Athletes Needing Lateral Strength and Stability
Tennis players, footballers, swimmers, and any athlete requiring lateral force production benefit from the lateral standing strength and hip stability Utthita Parsvakonasana develops.

Is Utthita Parsvakonasana Good for Beginners?
Yes — the forearm-on-thigh modification is accessible from early in yoga practice and immediately delivers the full lateral stretch, hip opening, and lower body strength benefits.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should I hold Utthita Parsvakonasana on each side?

Hold for 5 to 10 breath cycles per side — approximately 30 to 
60 seconds
. Build to 60 to 
90 seconds
 as lower body strength develops. Three rounds per side with adequate rest between is the standard practice structure. Breathing steadily through the lateral hold is both the physical and mental training the pose provides.

Why is my chest facing the floor rather than the ceiling?

Chest falling toward the floor is the most common alignment error in Utthita Parsvakonasana — indicating the torso is not rotating sufficiently. Actively rotate the chest toward the ceiling by drawing the upper shoulder back and lifting the upper arm. If the chest cannot rotate open, use the forearm-on-thigh modification which makes the rotation more accessible.

Can Utthita Parsvakonasana help with waist toning?

Yes — the sustained lateral oblique engagement — inner obliques compressing on the bent-knee side, outer obliques stretching on the upper side — directly tones the obliques and waist over consistent practice. The metabolic demand of the full held stance additionally supports body composition goals.

What is the correct progression from forearm-on-thigh to hand on block to floor?

Begin with the forearm resting on the front thigh — this is accessible from the first session. Once the chest rotation and knee tracking are established, progress to a block at the highest height outside the front foot. As hamstring and hip flexibility develops, lower the block height. Floor contact with a flat back is the final stage, developed over weeks to months.

Is Utthita Parsvakonasana good for athletes?

Yes — particularly for tennis, football, basketball, and swimming. The lateral hip strength, groin flexibility, and oblique conditioning developed through the wide-legged bent-knee lateral stance are directly transferable to the lateral force production and change of direction that most sports require.

Can I practice Utthita Parsvakonasana every day?

Yes — with adequate warm-up. The standing strength demand means it benefits from the preparatory heat of Surya Namaskara and Virabhadrasana 2 before being practiced. Daily practice produces rapid and meaningful improvement in lateral body flexibility, lower body strength, and hip opening within 1 to 2 weeks.

Who should not practice Utthita Parsvakonasana?

Those with acute knee ligament injuries or severe hip conditions should modify — the forearm-on-thigh variation significantly reduces the knee and hip demand. Those with acute lumbar disc herniation should avoid the lateral tilt until cleared by a doctor. Most practitioners can safely practice the forearm modification from the first session.
