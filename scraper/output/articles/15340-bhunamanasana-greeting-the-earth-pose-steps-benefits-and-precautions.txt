What is Bhunamanasana?

Bhunamanasana — pronounced bhoo-nah-mah-NAH-sah-nah — comes from the Sanskrit bhu (earth), namana (salutation or bowing) and asana (pose). The English translation, Greeting the Earth Pose or Saluting the Earth Pose, captures the essential gesture of the 
posture
: a deep, complete bow toward the ground that combines spinal rotation, hip opening and full surrender of the anterior body. It is one of the most symbolically rich poses in Hatha yoga — an act of humility and gratitude toward the earth that supports all life.

Bhunamanasana is classified as a prone twisting and hip-opening pose that produces a unique combination of effects not easily replicated by other postures. The wide-legged position opens the inner thighs and adductors; the lateral reaching of one arm beneath the body creates a thoracic rotation that few other prone poses achieve; and the complete lowering of the head toward the floor in the full expression creates cervical traction alongside the trunk rotation. The bhunamanasana benefits are therefore spread across multiple body regions simultaneously.

In the broader yoga system, Bhunamanasana is often placed within restorative, hip-opening or spinal health sequences. It shares elements with Parivrtta 
Janu Sirsasana
 (Revolved Head-to-Knee), Thread the Needle and 
Wide-Legged Forward Fold
 — making it both a stand-alone practice and a complement to these poses within a comprehensive sequence.

The combined greeting the earth pose benefits for posterior chain release make it an effective single-pose recovery sequence element.

How to Do Bhunamanasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Bhunamanasana requires patience with the hip opening — force should never be applied to the inner thighs or groins. Allow gravity and breath to deepen the pose progressively over the hold duration. The thoracic rotation component should feel releasing, not compressive. Never force the shoulder or neck in the arm reach phase.

Step 1: Starting Position — Wide-Legged Standing

Stand with the feet wide apart — approximately 3-4 feet, or the distance at which the inner thighs feel a moderate stretch without strain. Turn the toes slightly outward (15-20 degrees). Establish equal weight through both feet and stand tall to lengthen the spine before initiating the forward movement.

Step 2: Forward Fold into Wide-Legged Position

Hinge forward from the hips — keeping the spine long — and place both hands on the floor between the feet. Allow the head to hang naturally or rest the crown of the head on the floor if accessible. Breathe slowly here for 3-5 breaths, allowing the inner thighs, hamstrings and lower back to release with each exhalation.

Step 3: Initiate the Spinal Rotation

Keeping the feet planted and the hips level, begin to rotate the thoracic spine to the right — lifting the right arm toward the ceiling if this is the Parivrtta variation, or threading the right arm beneath the body for the classic Bhunamanasana. The rotation should feel like a gentle wringing of the thoracic spine, not a forced movement.

Step 4: Thread the Arm and Lower the Shoulder

Thread the right arm beneath the body, reaching it out to the left — allowing the right shoulder and the right side of the head to lower toward the floor. The right palm faces upward. Allow gravity to draw the shoulder progressively lower without forcing. The left hand remains on the floor for initial stability.

Step 5: Final Position and Hold

In the full expression of Bhunamanasana, the right shoulder and right cheek rest on the floor with the right arm extended to the left — the complete “greeting the earth” gesture. The left arm can remain on the floor for support or reach overhead for additional opening. Hold 5-10 slow breaths, allowing the rotation to deepen passively with each exhalation.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Bhunamanasana

To release, press the left palm into the floor and use the arm to assist lifting the right shoulder back to centre. Return both hands to the floor between the feet and breathe for 2-3 breaths to re-centre before performing the pose on the opposite side. Always perform equal time on both sides to maintain spinal balance.

Breathing in Bhunamanasana

Slow diaphragmatic breathing deepens Bhunamanasana progressively — each inhalation creates space in the thoracic cage; each exhalation allows the shoulder to sink a little closer to the floor and the rotation to naturally deepen. The prone orientation naturally encourages deeper belly breathing. Never hold the breath in this pose.

Preparatory Poses Before Bhunamanasana

Prasarita Padottanasana (Wide-Legged Forward Fold) — opens the inner thighs and hamstrings in the same base position.

Thread the Needle — introduces the shoulder-and-arm threading movement in a supported four-point kneeling position.

Parivritta Trikonasana (Revolved Triangle) — prepares the thoracic rotation in a standing position before the deeper prone version.

Baddha Konasana (Bound Angle) — opens the hip external rotators required for the wide-legged base.

Variations of Bhunamanasana

Variation 1: Supported Bhunamanasana (Beginner)

Place a folded blanket under the shoulder that is lowering to the floor — reducing the range required for the shoulder contact. This allows the practitioner to experience the thoracic rotation without the full range that forces shoulder discomfort. Difficulty: Beginner

Variation 2: Arm Extended Overhead (Intermediate)

In the final position, extend the free arm overhead along the floor — creating a full lateral body stretch alongside the rotation. This variation increases the stretch along the entire side body and adds the shoulder anterior capsule opening of the overhead reach. Difficulty: Intermediate

Variation 3: Full Bhunamanasana with Cheek on Floor (Advanced)

The classical full expression with the cheek fully on the floor and the threading arm extended as far as possible — requiring significant thoracic rotation range and inner thigh flexibility. This is the complete greeting the earth pose as described in traditional texts. Difficulty: Advanced

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Bhunamanasana

Mistake 1: Forcing the Shoulder to the Floor
Using the free arm or body weight to force the threading shoulder to the floor before adequate rotation range exists creates anterior shoulder impingement. Allow gravity and breath to progressively deepen the shoulder descent over the hold duration.

Mistake 2: Lifting the Hips During Rotation
The hips should remain level throughout Bhunamanasana — rising on the rotating side defeats the thoracic rotation purpose. If the hip lifts, reduce the rotation range until hip stability can be maintained.

Mistake 3: Unequal Time on Both Sides
Spending unequal time on the right and left sides creates the spinal imbalance that Bhunamanasana is intended to address. Always time both sides equally — 5 breaths right, 5 breaths left.

Mistake 4: Holding the Breath
Breath holding prevents the progressive deepening that makes Bhunamanasana effective. Slow continuous breathing is essential for the passive release mechanism that produces the greeting the earth pose benefits.
