Parsvottanasana, the Intense Side Stretch Pose, is a classical standing forward bend that lengthens the hamstrings, opens the chest, stabilises the hips bilaterally, and calms the nervous system. Accessible to beginners with blocks and rewarding for intermediate practitioners refining alignment, its benefits compound with consistent daily practice.

What is Parsvottanasana?

Parsvottanasana (pronounced 
parsh-voh-tahn-AH-sah-nah
) is a classical standing forward-bend asana whose Sanskrit name combines three roots: 
parsva
 (side), 
ut
 (intense), and 
tan
 (to stretch). In English it is widely known as the 
Intense Side Stretch Pose
 or, informally, the 
Pyramid Pose
 — a name inspired by the sharp triangular silhouette the body forms when viewed from the side. The full 
parsvottanasana benefits
 become accessible only when the correct foundation is understood from the outset.

Visually, the pose resembles a deliberate bow over one extended leg: the feet are staggered, the hips square to the front, and the torso folds forward until the chest approaches the thigh. Arms are classically held in reverse namaste — palms pressed together behind the back — though blocks or an opposite-elbow hold work equally well for those building up. The resulting shape — straight back leg, deeply folded torso, grounded feet — is precisely where the pyramid analogy originates.

Within the broader yoga system, Parsvottanasana belongs to the family of asymmetrical standing poses that build bilateral balance, deep posterior-chain flexibility, and spinal awareness simultaneously. It appears in the Ashtanga Yoga Primary Series and is referenced in B.K.S. Iyengar’s foundational texts as a pose that simultaneously demands and develops hamstring length, hip mobility, and spinal extension. Traditionally, the deep bow of the torso carries a quality of deliberate humility combined with the physical steadiness of a fully grounded stance.

How to Do Parsvottanasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Before moving through the steps, keep three principles in mind: (1) 
hips square before you fold
 — do not let the back hip swing forward; (2) 
spine long before it tips
 — lead with the chest, not the head; and (3) 
back foot firmly planted
 — the outer edge of the back foot presses into the mat for the full duration of the pose. These three cues govern every transition below.

Step 1: Starting Position

Stand in Tadasana at the top of your mat. Feet are together, spine tall, shoulders relaxed. Take a few steady breaths here to bring awareness to the body before moving. Feel the four corners of both feet pressing evenly into the floor.

Step 2: Step the Feet Apart

Step your right foot back approximately 90–100 cm, turning it out at roughly 45–60 degrees. Your left foot remains pointing straight forward. Press the outer edge of your back foot firmly into the mat — this is your anchor for the entire pose. Both feet should be on the same width line rather than on a tightrope, which helps keep the hips square.

Step 3: Square the Hips and Form Reverse Namaste

Draw the right hip forward and the left hip back until both hip points face the front of the mat evenly. Simultaneously, bring both arms behind you and press the palms together in reverse prayer — fingers pointing upward between the shoulder blades. If the wrists are tight, hold opposite elbows instead. Feel the chest open and the shoulder blades gently draw together.

Step 4: Inhale and Extend the Spine

On an inhale, lift through the crown of the head, lengthen the sides of the waist, and draw the lower ribs in and up. You may feel a mild lift across the chest and a gentle opening along the front of the spine. This extension moment is crucial — it sets the spine up to fold with length rather than collapsing under load.

Step 5: Final Position and Hold

On an exhale, hinge at the hips and fold the torso forward and down over the front left leg. Lead with the chest — keep the spine long as it tips forward. Allow the torso to descend as far as your hamstring flexibility permits without rounding the lower back. If you can, bring the chest toward the shin or rest the forehead on the shin. Hold here for 5–8 steady breaths, keeping both legs straight and the hips level throughout.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Parsvottanasana

On an inhale, press firmly into both feet and lead with the chest to rise back to an upright position — do not push up with the back rounding. Once upright, release the reverse namaste, step the back foot forward to meet the front, and return to Tadasana. Pause for two breaths, then repeat on the other side with the left foot stepping back.

Breathing in Parsvottanasana

The breath pattern follows the principle common to most standing forward bends: 
inhale to lengthen
 (Step 4), 
exhale to fold
 (Step 5). Once in the final shape, breathe smoothly and evenly — each inhale creates a little more length through the spine, each exhale allows the torso to settle slightly deeper over the front leg. Avoid holding the breath in the fold, as this creates unnecessary tension in the hamstrings and lower back.

Preparatory Poses Before Parsvottanasana

These four poses warm up the muscle groups most active in Parsvottanasana — hamstrings, hip flexors, calves, and the thoracic spine. Spend 4–6 breaths in each before attempting the main pose.

Uttanasana (Standing Forward Fold)
 — Opens the hamstrings symmetrically before asking them to work asymmetrically in the full pose.

Virabhadrasana I (Warrior I)
 — Warms up the hip flexors and trains the action of squaring the hips over a staggered stance, which is the same base position as Parsvottanasana.

Adho Mukha Svanasana (Downward-Facing Dog)
 — Lengthens the calves and hamstrings while building shoulder stability needed for the reverse namaste arm position.

Gomukhasana arms (seated)
 — Prepares the shoulder joints and wrists for the reverse namaste grip. Working the arms separately here prevents strain in the full pose.

Variations of Parsvottanasana

Variation 1: Hands on Blocks (Beginner-Friendly)

Difficulty: Beginner.
 Place two yoga blocks — one on either side of the front foot — at their highest height. Instead of reverse namaste, rest the fingertips or palms on the blocks as you fold forward. This variation allows you to maintain a long, extended spine even with limited hamstring flexibility, making the core parsvottanasana benefits accessible from the very first session.

Variation 2: Ardha Parsvottanasana (Half Version)

Difficulty: Beginner to Intermediate.
 Rather than folding until the torso is parallel to the floor or beyond, bring the torso only halfway down — roughly at a 45-degree angle — with the spine fully extended. Hold here for your full breath count. This half version is especially useful for those with tighter hamstrings or recent lower-back discomfort, as it maintains spinal length without the depth that can provoke rounding.

Variation 3: Parsva Uttanasana with Arm Extension (Intermediate)

Difficulty: Intermediate.
 Instead of reverse namaste, extend both arms alongside the ears as you fold forward, reaching the fingertips toward the front foot or the floor. This intensifies the stretch along the side body and intercostal muscles, and suits practitioners who have shoulder mobility restrictions that prevent reverse namaste but still want the full depth of the forward fold.

Variation 4: Deep Fold with Chin to Shin (Advanced)

Difficulty: Advanced.
 With fully straight legs, squared hips, and a long spine, fold deeply enough to rest the chin on the shin of the front leg and wrap the hands around the back of the ankle or heel. This requires considerable hamstring and hip-flexor length. Only attempt this variation once the intermediate form feels stable and effortless over several weeks of consistent practice.

Who Should Practise Parsvottanasana?

Make Parsvottanasana a Part of Your Life

Parsvottanasana — the Intense Side Stretch or Pyramid Pose — is a standing forward bend that systematically lengthens the hamstrings, opens the chest, stabilises the hips, and builds the quiet mental focus that carries over into everyday life. It suits beginners working with props, intermediates refining alignment, and regular practitioners deepening a core standing-sequence pose.

Whether you are a complete beginner using blocks, someone managing back or hip stiffness, or an experienced practitioner working toward the deep fold, the right guidance removes the guesswork. Modifications exist for every starting point, and real-time feedback from a teacher who can see your body is what distinguishes a pose that feels safe and effective from one that quietly reinforces the wrong pattern.
