Astavakrasana — the Eight-Angle Pose — is an advanced arm balance that requires simultaneous arm strength, core stability, and hip flexibility to hold the body suspended with both legs extended horizontally to one side. Named after the sage Astavakra — whose body was bent at eight points — the pose embodies the transformation of apparent limitation into graceful, balanced expression. It is accessible through patient, systematic preparation and is one of the most rewarding arm balances in the Hatha yoga system.

What is Astavakrasana?

Astavakrasana — pronounced ash-tah-vak-RAHS-ana — translates as Eight-Angle Pose (Ashta = eight, Vakra = bent or curved, Asana = posture). The name honours the sage Astavakra, who was born with eight bends in his body — a physical condition that did not prevent him from becoming one of the most profound philosophical teachers in the Vedic tradition. The pose is a metaphor: the body arranged at multiple angles, all held in balance through inner strength and focused awareness.

In the astavakrasana yoga pose, the practitioner plants both hands shoulder-width apart, hooks one leg above the corresponding elbow, crosses the other leg over, and extends both legs horizontally to one side — the arms bearing the complete body weight while the core maintains the horizontal suspension. It belongs to the same arm balance family as Bakasana (Crow Pose) — though Astavakrasana’s lateral leg extension creates a distinctly different balance challenge. Astavakrasana Benefits

Physical Benefits

Builds Exceptional Arm and Wrist Strength
Astavakrasana demands sustained pressing strength through the wrists, forearms, and upper arms — the complete body weight supported through the arms in a held isometric contraction. This arm strengthening is directly complemented by the foundational pressing practice of

Chaturanga Dandasana, which builds the tricep and shoulder girdle strength that Astavakrasana’s lateral suspension requires. Regular practice progressively builds the upper body strength that this and all subsequent arm balances depend on.
Develops Deep Core Strength and Lateral Stability

Holding the body extended horizontally requires sustained deep core engagement — particularly the obliques and transverse abdominis that govern lateral spinal stability. This core development is an excellent complement to the frontal core work of
Navasana (Boat Pose), creating a complete core conditioning programme that addresses frontal, lateral, and rotational strength simultaneously.

Improves Hip Flexibility and Leg-Threading Mobility
The leg threading entry of Astavakrasana — hooking one leg above the elbow and crossing the ankles — requires and develops meaningful hip flexor and external rotator flexibility. This progressive hip opening is part of the broader

yoga for flexibility benefits that consistent arm balance practice produces across the complete hip and hamstring complex.
Strengthens the Serratus Anterior and Shoulder Girdle

The protracted, depressed scapular position that sustains Astavakrasana activates the serratus anterior — building the shoulder girdle stability that prevents scapular winging and protects the shoulder joint in all arm-supported postures.
Supports Spinal Awareness and Postural Health

Astavakrasana’s demand for precise spinal alignment in the arm balance develops the postural body awareness that translates into improved everyday posture. This spinal awareness is a meaningful complement to
yoga for back pain practice — the strengthened posterior chain and improved body awareness supporting the spinal health that back pain management requires.

Mental and Emotional Benefits

Builds Fearless Confidence and Courage
Committing body weight to the arms in Astavakrasana requires a specific quality of fearless trust — the willingness to lift completely off the floor with no other support. This courage, consistently developed through progressive arm balance practice, builds the confident, self-trusting quality that practitioners consistently report in daily life alongside their physical development.

Develops Intense Single-Pointed Concentration
Astavakrasana’s multiple simultaneous demands — arm strength, core engagement, balance, and breathing — create a practice that requires complete, undivided concentration. The mind cannot wander in Astavakrasana; the totality of the body’s engagement demands total mental presence.

Cultivates Equanimity Through Physical Challenge
Named after a sage who transcended physical limitation through philosophical mastery, Astavakrasana invites the practitioner to find ease within challenge — the calm, equanimous presence within the physical demand that advanced yoga practice uniquely develops.

How to Do Astavakrasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

Astavakrasana must be entered through the correct leg-threading sequence — never by jumping or swinging into position. The weight is borne through the full palm, not the wrist alone: spread the fingers and distribute the load. The gaze stays forward throughout — looking down collapses the chest and disrupts balance. Exit is always controlled and deliberate.

Step 1: Establish the Starting Position
Sit on the floor with both legs extended forward. Bend the right knee and lift the right leg — drawing the right thigh as high toward the right armpit as flexibility allows.

Step 2: Thread the Right Arm and Hook the Leg
Thread the right arm between the right thigh and calf. Hook the right leg as high above the right elbow as possible — ideally at the shoulder level. Plant the right palm firmly on the floor.

Step 3: Cross the Ankles and Plant Both Hands
Cross the left ankle over the right ankle — the left foot locking the right leg in position. Plant the left palm firmly on the floor shoulder-width from the right. Spread all ten fingers and press evenly through the full palm.

Step 4: Lean Forward and Lift the Hips
Lean the body weight forward over the hands. Bend both elbows — the upper arms creating a shelf for the thighs. On an exhale, engage the core strongly and lift the hips off the floor. The feet will follow as the weight transfers to the arms.

Step 5: Extend Both Legs and Hold
Straighten both arms as much as possible. Extend both legs to the right — squeezing the thighs toward each other to maintain the hook above the elbow. The body is now suspended horizontally on the arms. Hold for 3–5 breath cycles, gaze steady forward.

Step 6: Return with Control
Bend the elbows slightly to lower the hips back to the floor with control. Release the ankle crossing. Unthread the right arm. Repeat on the left side for balanced practice — left leg hooked above the left elbow, legs extending to the left.

Breathing in Astavakrasana

The lift into Astavakrasana is initiated on a strong exhale — the core engagement that empties the lungs providing the abdominal activation that raises the hips. Once suspended, breathe steadily and evenly through the nose. The breath will be somewhat compressed by the body’s position — breathe into the sides of the ribcage. Never hold the breath in the held position; the steady breath maintains the parasympathetic composure that Astavakrasana demands.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Astavakrasana?

Astavakrasana is the Eight-Angle Pose — an advanced arm balance in which both legs extend horizontally to one side with the body suspended on the arms. One leg hooks above the elbow while the ankles cross to lock the position. It is practised on both sides and named after the eight-jointed sage Astavakra.

What are the benefits of Astavakrasana?

Astavakrasana builds exceptional arm and wrist strength, develops deep lateral core stability, improves hip flexibility through the threading entry, strengthens the serratus anterior and shoulder girdle, and cultivates the fearless concentration and equanimity that advanced arm balance practice develops. These benefits are progressive and compound with consistent practice.

How do you do Astavakrasana step by step?

Thread the right arm through the right leg and hook the leg above the elbow. Cross the left ankle over the right. Plant both palms firmly and spread the fingers. Lean forward, bend the elbows, engage the core, and lift the hips on an exhale. Straighten the arms and extend both legs to the right. Hold 3–5 breaths. Lower with control. Repeat on the left side.

Is Astavakrasana suitable for beginners?

Astavakrasana is intermediate to advanced in difficulty and requires prerequisite arm strength and hip flexibility that beginners typically need several months to develop. Beginners should build comfort in Chaturanga Dandasana, Plank, and Bakasana before approaching Astavakrasana. Habuild’s live instructors guide the progression appropriately from each practitioner’s current level.

How is Astavakrasana different from Bakasana?

Bakasana (Crow Pose) is a compact, forward-leaning arm balance with the knees resting on the upper arms and the weight balanced over the hands. Astavakrasana extends both legs horizontally to one side — requiring the specific leg-threading entry, lateral core strength, and greater arm endurance of the extended, suspended position. Bakasana is typically learned first and provides the arm balance foundation that Astavakrasana builds upon.
