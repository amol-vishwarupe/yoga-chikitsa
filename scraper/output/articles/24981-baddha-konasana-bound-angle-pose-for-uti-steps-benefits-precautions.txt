What is Baddha Konasana?

Baddha Konasana (pronounced BAH-dah cone-AHS-anna) comes from Sanskrit: 
baddha
 means bound, 
kona
 means angle, and 
asana
 means posture. In English it is commonly called Bound Angle Pose, Butterfly Pose, or Cobbler’s Pose — the last name reflecting the cross-legged sitting position traditionally used by cobblers in India. The pose involves sitting upright with the soles of the feet pressed together and the knees falling open to either side, creating a diamond shape with the legs.

Symbolically, Baddha Konasana is associated with grounded openness — the body simultaneously rooted through the pelvis and open through the hips. In traditional yogic physiology, the pelvic region is considered the seat of 
apana vayu
, the downward-flowing energy responsible for elimination, reproductive function, and detoxification. Poses that stimulate and release this area are considered foundational to pelvic wellness, making Baddha Konasana a natural choice when exploring yoga asanas for urine infection support.

Within the broader yoga system, Baddha Konasana belongs to the category of seated hip-opening asanas. It appears in Hatha yoga sequences, Iyengar practice, Yin yoga, and restorative yoga traditions. Its accessibility — requiring no equipment and adaptable to all levels — makes it one of the most universally practised poses in the global yoga community.

How to Do Baddha Konasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Move through this sequence gently, especially if you are currently experiencing active infection symptoms. The goal is release, not intensity.

Key Principles

Always practise on an empty or light stomach. Place a folded blanket under your sitting bones if your hips are tight — this small adjustment tilts the pelvis forward and protects the lower back. Never push through sharp pelvic pain. Keep the breath slow and even throughout; if it becomes laboured, ease out of the pose immediately.

Step 1: Starting Position

Sit on your mat with both legs extended straight in front of you — this is Dandasana, or Staff Pose. Place your palms flat beside your hips, press gently into the mat, and grow tall through your spine from the base of the pelvis to the crown of your head. Take three slow, full breaths here, feeling your sitting bones connect with the floor. This grounding moment primes the nervous system for the deeper pelvic work ahead.

Step 2: Drawing the Heels In

Bend both knees and draw the soles of your feet together, allowing the knees to fall open to either side like the wings of a butterfly. Draw your heels as close to your pelvis as is comfortable — never force this distance. Wrap your hands around your feet or ankles and sit tall. You should feel a gentle stretch across the inner thighs and groin, not pain or strain.

Step 3: Engaging the Spine

On an inhale, lengthen through the crown of your head as if a thread were pulling you upward. On each exhale, consciously soften the inner thighs and allow the knees to release a little further toward the floor — not by pressing with your hands, but by releasing the hip flexors. Keep the shoulders relaxed away from the ears and the lower back long rather than rounded. This alignment protects the lumbar spine while maximising the benefit to the pelvic region.

Step 4: The Optional Forward Fold

Only if you are not in acute discomfort — hinge forward from your hips, maintaining a long spine rather than rounding through the back. Lower your chest slowly toward your feet. This deepens the stimulation to the pelvic and abdominal organs and increases the inner-thigh stretch. Hold for three to five full breaths. If you feel any sharp pain in the lower abdomen, come back upright immediately.

Step 5: Final Position and Hold

Whether upright or in a gentle fold, hold the pose for five to ten complete breaths. With each exhale, consciously soften and release the pelvic floor — imagine any gripping or holding in the lower belly dissolving with the breath out. This deliberate release is the most therapeutically valuable moment of the entire pose and is central to the yoga for UTI relief approach.

Step 6: How to Come Out of Baddha Konasana

On an inhale, slowly bring your spine back to upright if you folded forward. Use both hands to gently guide your knees back toward centre, then extend both legs in front of you, returning to Dandasana. Sit here for two to three breaths before moving to the next pose or lying down in Savasana. The exit is part of the practice — never rush it.

Breathing in Baddha Konasana

Throughout the entire sequence, aim for a 4-count inhale and a 6-count exhale. The longer exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system, which is central to the stress-reduction benefit of yoga for UTI relief. Avoid breath retention during active infection, as holding the breath can create unnecessary pressure in the abdominal cavity.

Preparatory Poses Before Baddha Konasana

These four poses warm up the hips, inner thighs, and lower back — the muscle groups most engaged during Baddha Konasana — and help you arrive at the pose with less tension and more receptivity.

Supta Baddha Konasana (Reclined Bound Angle Pose):
 Lie on your back with soles together and knees falling open. A completely passive version of the main pose — it relaxes the pelvic floor before you ask it to engage.

Marjaryasana-Bitilasana (Cat-Cow):
 Gently mobilises the lumbar spine and massages the abdominal organs, preparing the body for deeper pelvic work without any load on the hips.

Viparita Karani (Legs Up the Wall):
 A mild inversion that encourages lymphatic drainage from the lower limbs and creates a calming effect before the main practice sequence.

Seated Hip Circles:
 Sitting cross-legged, make slow smooth circles with your upper body to warm the hip joints and sacral area gently — no load, no strain, just movement.

Variations of Baddha Konasana

Variation 1: Supta Baddha Konasana (Reclined Version — Beginner Level)

Lie flat on your back and draw the soles of your feet together, allowing the knees to fall open toward the floor. Place a folded blanket under each knee if the inner thighs feel strained. This is the most accessible entry point — ideal when experiencing active discomfort or when you are completely new to the pose. It delivers all the pelvic-floor release benefits of the seated version with zero spinal load, making it a first-choice yoga asana for urine infection discomfort during recovery.

Variation 2: Parsva Baddha Konasana (Side Bend — Intermediate Level)

From the upright seated position, walk your right hand out to the right side and let your torso lean gently in that direction while extending your left arm overhead. This lateral variation stretches the obliques and the side of the lower abdomen, adding a gentle lymphatic dimension to the core pose. Hold for five breaths, then switch sides.

Variation 3: Baddha Konasana with Forward Fold and Block Support (Intermediate Level)

Place a yoga block or firm cushion beneath your forehead when folding forward. This supported variation allows the spine to relax completely into the fold without any muscular effort to hold the head up — deepening the parasympathetic response while maintaining gentle organ stimulation. It is particularly useful for practitioners who hold tension in the neck and shoulders during forward bends.

Variation 4: Dynamic Butterfly (Restorative — All Levels)

From the basic seated Baddha Konasana position, gently flap the knees up and down like butterfly wings in a slow, rhythmic motion — eight to ten repetitions. This dynamic version warms the inner thighs without static holding, making it suitable as both a warm-up before the static pose and a release movement after a longer hold.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Baddha Konasana

Practising During Acute, Severe Infection

Baddha Konasana can support recovery but is not a substitute for medical care. If you have a fever, severe burning, blood in urine, or pain radiating toward the kidneys, rest and seek medical attention before practising. Begin gentle yoga only once acute symptoms have started to ease under appropriate treatment.

Forcing the Knees Toward the Floor

Pushing the knees down with your hands creates tension rather than release — the opposite of what this pose intends. Focus on consciously softening the inner thighs on each exhale. With consistent practice, the knees will naturally descend as the hip flexors lengthen over time.

Rounding the Lower Back

Collapsing the lumbar spine compresses the pelvic organs rather than stimulating them beneficially. Sit on a folded blanket to tilt the pelvis forward and maintain the natural lumbar curve throughout the pose. This single adjustment transforms the experience of the posture significantly.

Holding the Breath

Breath retention increases intra-abdominal pressure, which is contraindicated when managing UTI discomfort. Keep the breath flowing smoothly at all times — the slow exhale is your most powerful tool in this practice.

Skipping the Cool-Down

Ending a pelvic yoga session abruptly without Savasana or a restorative close means the nervous system does not complete its transition into a parasympathetic state. Always allow at least five minutes of stillness at the end of your practice.

Combining with Intense Core Work

High-intensity abdominal exercises create significant downward pressure on the lower abdomen. During UTI recovery, keep the entire practice gentle and release-focused. Save core strengthening work for when you are fully recovered.

Frequently Asked Questions About Baddha Konasana

What is Baddha Konasana?

Baddha Konasana is a seated hip-opening asana from the Hatha yoga tradition in which the soles of the feet are pressed together and the knees fall open to either side. Known in English as Bound Angle Pose or Butterfly Pose, it is one of the most effective yoga poses for UTI support because it directly stimulates the pelvic floor, improves circulation to the lower abdomen, and encourages a conscious release of pelvic tension — all while remaining accessible to practitioners of every level.

Is Baddha Konasana good for beginners?

Yes — Baddha Konasana is one of the most beginner-friendly yoga asanas available. The reclined variation, Supta Baddha Konasana, requires no prior flexibility and can be practised from day one. Beginners should start lying down, then gradually work toward the seated version as their inner thigh and hip flexibility improves.

What is the difference between Baddha Konasana and Hatha yoga?

Hatha yoga is a broad and comprehensive system that encompasses postures, breathwork, cleansing practices, and meditation. Baddha Konasana is one specific asana within that system — a seated hip opener. When people explore yoga asanas for urine infection relief, they are typically selecting a curated set of poses from within the larger Hatha framework, chosen specifically for their effect on the pelvic floor and urinary organs.
