Hasta Uttanasana — the Raised Arms Pose — is the second and eleventh pose in the classical Surya Namaskar (Sun Salutation) sequence and one of the most fundamental standing backbends in yoga. The hasta uttanasana yoga pose opens the chest, stretches the anterior body from fingertips to toes, improves thoracic extension, and sets the energetic tone of upward expansion that characterises the complete Surya Namaskar practice.

What is Hasta Uttanasana?

Hasta Uttanasana — pronounced HAH-stah oo-tah-NAH-sana — translates as Raised Arms Pose (Hasta = hands, Uttana = intense stretch, Asana = pose). It is also called Urdhva Hastasana (Raised Hands Pose) in some traditions, and its placement as the opening movement of Surya Namaskar makes it one of the most practised yoga positions in the world.

In the hasta uttanasana yoga pose, the practitioner stands in Tadasana and sweeps both arms overhead — palms pressing together or facing each other — creating a gentle thoracic backbend as the arms rise. The gaze follows the thumbs upward and slightly back. The stretch extends from the fingertips through the lateral body, chest, and anterior thighs down to the feet.

Hasta Uttanasana Benefits

Physical Benefits

Opens the Chest and Improves Thoracic Extension
Hasta Uttanasana directly counteracts the forward-rounded chest and thoracic kyphosis of desk posture — the raised arms and gentle back-bend opening the anterior chest, stretching the pectorals, and restoring the thoracic extension that modern sitting progressively eliminates.

Stretches the Anterior Body from Fingertips to Toes
The full hasta uttanasana benefits for the anterior chain include lengthening the lateral torso, stretching the intercostal spaces, opening the abdomen, and creating traction through the entire front body from hands to feet.

Improves Shoulder Mobility and Overhead Range of Motion
The arms reaching overhead in Hasta Uttanasana develops the shoulder flexion range of motion and the scapular upward rotation that healthy shoulder function requires — directly relevant for all overhead yoga poses and daily activities.

Stimulates the Digestive Organs and Improves Posture
The abdominal stretch of the raised-arms backbend stimulates the digestive organs through extension — complementing the compression stimulation of forward folds with an extension-based digestive activation.

Mental and Emotional Benefits

Cultivates an Upward, Expansive Energy and Positive Outlook
The physical gesture of arms raised overhead toward the sky mirrors the mental quality of openness, expansion, and receptivity. Practitioners consistently describe Hasta Uttanasana as a pose that immediately lifts mood and creates a sense of energetic spaciousness.

Prepares the Mind and Body for Practice
As the second position of Surya Namaskar, Hasta Uttanasana signals the beginning of intentional practice — the body opening upward and the mind turning inward together. Its placement in the sequence makes it a daily ritual of preparation and intention.

How to Do Hasta Uttanasana — Step-by-Step Instructions

Key Principles

The backbend is thoracic — not lumbar. The lower ribs do not flare forward. The neck stays long — the gaze follows the hands without cramping the cervical spine. The arms remain shoulder-width if the shoulders are tight — palms can touch overhead only if the shoulders allow it without tension.

Step 1: Stand in Tadasana
Stand in Tadasana with feet together, spine tall, arms beside the body. Establish the breath.

Step 2: Inhale and Sweep the Arms Outward and Upward
Inhale and sweep both arms out to the sides and upward — the arms tracing a wide arc from the sides to directly overhead.

Step 3: Bring the Palms Together or Parallel Overhead
As the arms reach overhead, bring the palms to touch (if the shoulders allow) or keep them parallel, facing each other. Arms are slightly in front of vertical.

Step 4: Gently Arch the Upper Back
Allow a gentle arch through the upper back — the sternum lifting upward, the chest opening toward the ceiling. The lower ribs stay in, the lumbar spine does not compress.

Step 5: Gaze Upward Toward the Thumbs
Turn the gaze upward — following the thumbs as the arms rise. The neck extends gently, the chin lifting without cramping the cervical spine.

Step 6: Hold and Return
Hold for 3–5 breaths or transition into the next Surya Namaskar movement. To return, exhale and sweep the arms back down.

Breathing in Hasta Uttanasana

Inhale to sweep the arms upward — the breath and the movement synchronised so the full inhalation corresponds to the full arm rise. Maintain deep inhalation throughout the hold. Exhale to return or transition. The breath is the primary driver of the pose.

Preparatory Poses Before Hasta Uttanasana

Tadasana (Mountain Pose, 5 breaths) — Establishes the upright spinal foundation before the arm raise.

Shoulder rolls and arm swings (10 rounds) — Warms the shoulder joint before the overhead extension.

Cobra Pose (Bhujangasana, 5 breaths) — Opens the anterior chest before the standing backbend.

Cat-Cow Flow (10 rounds) — Warms the thoracic spine for the gentle backbend.

Variations of Hasta Uttanasana

Variation 1: Hasta Uttanasana with Shoulder-Width Arms — Accessible
Keep the arms parallel shoulder-width rather than bringing the palms together — this accessible variation removes the shoulder flexibility demand and allows the chest opening to occur without shoulder tension.

Variation 2: Hasta Uttanasana with Interlaced Fingers — Intermediate
Interlace the fingers and turn the palms upward overhead — the reversed grip creating a different shoulder and thoracic stretch quality that opens the lateral body more intensely.

Variation 3: Hasta Uttanasana as Dynamic Flow — Surya Namaskar
Practised as the second and eleventh position of Surya Namaskar, Hasta Uttanasana becomes a dynamic breath-synchronised movement — the inhale and arm rise forming one unified action that generates the heat and energy of the Sun Salutation sequence.

Common Mistakes to Avoid in Hasta Uttanasana

Arching from the lower back instead of the thoracic spine — The backbend of Hasta Uttanasana comes from the thoracic spine. Lower back arching compresses the lumbar discs and should be avoided.

Lower ribs flaring forward — The lower ribs stay in and down as the arms rise. Flaring ribs indicates a lumbar rather than thoracic backbend.

Cramping the back of the neck — The neck extends gently — the chin lifts, the gaze follows the thumbs, but the back of the neck stays long and uncramped.

Holding the breath during the hold — Hasta Uttanasana is an inhale-based pose — the breath must remain full and expansive throughout. Breath-holding reduces the chest-opening and energetic benefits.

Bringing the palms together when the shoulders are tight — Forcing the palms together when the shoulders are not open enough creates neck and shoulder tension. Keep the arms shoulder-width until the shoulder flexibility allows a comfortable touch.

Everyone Practising Surya Namaskar

As the second position of the Sun Salutation, Hasta Uttanasana is practised by every yoga practitioner performing Surya Namaskar — making correct technique in this foundational pose essential for the entire sequence’s integrity.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Hasta Uttanasana?

Hasta Uttanasana is the Raised Arms Pose — the second and eleventh position of Surya Namaskar in which both arms sweep overhead with a gentle thoracic backbend, the palms pressing together or parallel, the gaze following the thumbs upward. It opens the chest, stretches the anterior body, and improves shoulder overhead range of motion.

What are the hasta uttanasana benefits?

The primary hasta uttanasana benefits are: chest and anterior chain opening, thoracic extension improvement, shoulder overhead mobility development, postural correction for rounded shoulders, digestive organ stimulation through extension, and the energetic uplift and mood improvement that the expansive raised-arms gesture produces.

What is the hasta uttanasana yoga pose in Surya Namaskar?

In Surya Namaskar, Hasta Uttanasana is the second position (from Tadasana) and the eleventh position (before returning to Tadasana). The inhale corresponds to the arm rise and the backbend — the breath and movement synchronised as one unified action that generates the heat and energy of the Sun Salutation.

How to do Hasta Uttanasana correctly?

Stand in Tadasana. Inhale and sweep the arms upward. Bring palms to touch or keep parallel overhead. Allow a gentle thoracic backbend — sternum lifting, lower ribs staying in. Turn the gaze upward toward the thumbs with the neck long. Hold for the full inhalation or transition into the next movement.

What is the difference between Hasta Uttanasana and Urdhva Hastasana?

The terms are largely interchangeable in modern practice — both refer to the raised arms overhead position. Hasta Uttanasana (intense hand stretch) is used specifically in the Surya Namaskar context, while Urdhva Hastasana (upward hands pose) is used as the standalone standing pose. The technique and benefits are identical.
